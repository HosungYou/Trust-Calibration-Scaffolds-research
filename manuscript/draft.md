## Abstract

The rapid integration of generative artificial intelligence into education has intensified questions about learner trust calibration --- whether learners trust AI systems appropriately given those systems' actual capabilities and limitations. Bastani et al. (2025) demonstrated that unrestricted access to AI tutoring can harm learning outcomes, with students showing 17% lower performance on subsequent unassisted assessments, a finding attributable to uncalibrated overtrust and consequent cognitive offloading. Despite the significance of this result, existing theoretical frameworks in educational AI research address trust formation and technology acceptance but not trust calibration --- the alignment between a learner's trust in an AI system and the system's actual reliability for a given task. This paper presents a critical review of the literature on trust in educational AI (2015--2026), drawing on Web of Science and Scopus databases, with thematic and gap analysis across trust conceptualizations, theoretical frameworks, measurement approaches, and oversight design. The review reveals a critical calibration gap: most studies treat trust as a unidimensional positive variable to be maximized rather than as a calibration target to be aligned with system capability, and virtually no studies integrate trust calibration with systematic oversight design. To address this gap, we propose the Two-Level Trust Calibration Framework, which integrates: (a) Level 1 --- Learner Trust Dynamics, describing how trust forms, updates, and miscalibrates through cognitive and metacognitive processes; (b) Level 2 --- System-Mediated Trust Calibration, specifying six trust calibration scaffolds grounded in established educational theory (metacognitive prompts, desirable difficulties, productive failure, AI uncertainty transparency, Socratic dialogue, and progressive autonomy release); connected through (c) an Adaptive Calibration Cycle aligned with self-regulated learning theory, in which scaffold intensity adjusts dynamically based on behavioral signals of learner trust calibration. The paper distinguishes trust calibration from self-regulated learning calibration, introduces the Trust-AI Reliability Matrix as a diagnostic tool for assessing learner trust states, and derives five design principles for trust-calibrated educational AI. Implications for system designers, educators, and researchers are discussed, along with a focused research agenda for empirical validation of the framework.

**Keywords:** trust calibration, educational AI, scaffolding, self-regulated learning, human oversight, generative AI
## 1. Introduction

The promise of artificial intelligence in education rests on a deceptively simple assumption: that giving learners access to capable AI tools will improve learning. A landmark study by Bastani et al. (2025), published in the *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences*, dismantled this assumption with striking empirical evidence. In a large-scale randomized controlled trial involving nearly a thousand students solving mathematics problems, the researchers found that unrestricted access to ChatGPT not only failed to improve learning outcomes but actively harmed them. Students who used ChatGPT without structured guidance performed 17% worse on subsequent assessments than those who received no AI assistance at all. The mechanism was not a failure of the AI's capability but a failure of the learners' trust calibration: students uncritically accepted AI-generated outputs, bypassing the effortful cognitive processing that constitutes genuine learning. When the researchers introduced a tutoring-oriented version of ChatGPT that withheld direct answers and instead guided students through problem-solving steps, the negative effects disappeared and modest learning gains emerged. This finding underscores a fundamental insight: the educational value of AI depends not on what the technology can do, but on how learners relate to it --- and specifically, on whether they trust it appropriately.

The rapid integration of generative AI into higher education has created what we term a *design dilemma of autonomy and oversight*. On one end, granting AI systems broad autonomy to assist learners risks fostering the kind of uncritical dependence that Bastani et al. (2025) documented. On the other, imposing rigid constraints on AI use risks undermining the flexibility and personalization that make AI-assisted learning potentially transformative (Kasneci et al., 2023; Mollick & Mollick, 2023). Navigating this dilemma requires understanding the mechanism that mediates between AI capability and learning outcomes --- a mechanism that current research has largely overlooked.

The educational AI literature has grown rapidly, with substantial bodies of work examining learning outcomes (Cotton et al., 2024; Lo, 2023), learner acceptance and adoption (Al-Adwan et al., 2023; Strzelecki, 2023), and pedagogical integration strategies (Baidoo-Anu & Ansah, 2023). Yet a critical variable remains undertheorized: learner trust. Trust is not merely an affective response --- a feeling of comfort or confidence toward AI --- but a *calibration problem*. Trust calibration refers to the degree of correspondence between a person's trust in a system and the system's actual capabilities and reliability (Lee & See, 2004). When trust is well calibrated, learners rely on AI when it is reliable and exercise independent judgment when it is not. When trust is miscalibrated, the consequences diverge depending on the direction of error. Overtrust --- where perceived AI capability exceeds actual capability --- leads to uncritical acceptance, cognitive offloading, and the learning degradation observed by Bastani et al. (2025). Undertrust --- where perceived capability falls below actual capability --- leads to the rejection of beneficial AI assistance and missed opportunities for scaffolded learning (Dzindolet et al., 2003; Parasuraman & Riley, 1997).

Despite the centrality of trust calibration to the success or failure of educational AI, existing research frameworks do not adequately address it. Technology acceptance models (Davis, 1989; Venkatesh et al., 2003) treat trust as a predictor of adoption rather than a dynamic state requiring ongoing calibration. Self-regulated learning (SRL) frameworks (Zimmerman, 2000; Winne & Hadwin, 1998) address how learners monitor and regulate their own cognition but do not extend to calibrating judgments about external AI systems. Even the recent Stimulus-Organism-Response (S-O-R) model proposed by Wang et al. (2025) for AI-assisted learning, while usefully distinguishing trust from reliance, stops at the individual learner level and does not address how educational systems can be designed to actively calibrate trust.

This paper addresses this gap through three contributions. First, we map the current literature landscape of trust in educational AI research through a critical review, identifying how trust is conceptualized, measured, and theoretically framed. Second, we diagnose the *calibration gap* in existing frameworks --- the absence of systematic attention to the alignment between learner trust and AI capability, and the lack of connection between learner-level trust dynamics and system-level design decisions. Third, we propose a Two-Level Trust Calibration Framework that bridges this gap by connecting micro-level learner trust dynamics with macro-level system-mediated trust calibration through an adaptive calibration cycle grounded in scaffolding theory.

Four research questions guide this inquiry:

- **RQ1:** How is trust in AI conceptualized and measured in educational AI research?
- **RQ2:** What theoretical frameworks guide trust research in educational AI contexts?
- **RQ3:** To what extent does existing research address trust calibration and the design of human oversight mechanisms in educational AI?
- **RQ4:** What does a two-level framework connecting learner trust dynamics to system-mediated trust calibration look like, and how can it inform the design of educational AI?

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section 2 establishes the conceptual foundation by synthesizing trust theory from automation research and translating it to educational contexts, with particular attention to the distinction between SRL calibration and trust calibration. Section 3 describes our critical review approach. Section 4 presents the findings of the literature review organized around RQ1--RQ3. Section 5 introduces the Two-Level Trust Calibration Framework in response to RQ4, detailing its components, mechanisms, and design principles. Section 6 discusses theoretical and practical implications, limitations, and directions for future research. Section 7 concludes by arguing that trust calibration represents the missing link in educational AI design --- one that must be addressed if the field is to move beyond the simplistic assumption that AI access equals learning improvement.
## 2. Conceptual Foundation

This section establishes the theoretical groundwork for the Two-Level Trust Calibration Framework. We begin by tracing the origins of trust research in automation and human factors, then translate these constructs to educational contexts. We introduce the critical distinction between self-regulated learning (SRL) calibration and trust calibration --- a distinction that reveals a significant blind spot in current educational AI research. We then examine the recent S-O-R model of Wang et al. (2025) as the most proximate theoretical antecedent, identifying both its contributions and its limitations. Finally, we preview the two-level framework that addresses these limitations.

### 2.1 Trust in Automation and Its Educational Translation

The study of trust between humans and automated systems has a rich intellectual history in human factors research, yielding constructs and frameworks that are directly relevant --- yet insufficiently translated --- to educational AI contexts. Lee and See (2004), in their seminal review, defined trust as "the attitude that an agent will help achieve an individual's goals in a situation characterized by uncertainty and vulnerability" (p. 54). This definition foregrounds two conditions that are acutely present in educational settings: *uncertainty* about whether an AI system's outputs are correct and pedagogically appropriate, and *vulnerability* because the learner's cognitive development is at stake. Lee and See further proposed that trust is shaped by three informational bases: *performance* (what the system does --- its competence and reliability), *process* (how the system operates --- its transparency and predictability), and *purpose* (why the system was designed --- its underlying intent and alignment with the user's goals). Each of these dimensions maps onto distinct concerns in educational AI: performance relates to the accuracy of AI-generated content, process relates to the explainability of AI reasoning, and purpose relates to whether the AI is designed to support genuine learning rather than simply provide answers.

Hoff and Bashir (2015) extended this foundation by proposing a three-layer model of trust in automation that accounts for individual differences and temporal dynamics. *Dispositional trust* captures stable personality-level tendencies toward trusting or distrusting technology, shaped by factors such as age, culture, and prior experience. *Situational trust* is context-dependent, influenced by the specific task environment, perceived risk, and the user's current cognitive and affective state. *Learned trust* develops through direct experience with a specific system, accumulating across interactions as the user observes the system's reliability. This temporal layering is particularly important in educational contexts, where trust evolves across an entire semester of interactions rather than during a single encounter.

Complementing these frameworks, Mayer et al. (1995) proposed the widely cited Ability-Benevolence-Integrity (ABI) model of organizational trust. Although originally developed for interpersonal relationships, the ABI model has been increasingly applied to human-technology interaction (Lankton et al., 2015). *Ability* refers to the trustee's competence within a specific domain; *benevolence* captures the perception that the trustee acts in the trustor's interest; and *integrity* concerns adherence to principles the trustor finds acceptable. In the context of educational AI, ability maps to the AI's domain-specific accuracy, benevolence maps to the perception that the AI is designed to support learning rather than to replace the learner's effort, and integrity maps to the AI's consistency and adherence to educational norms. De Visser et al. (2020) further contributed the concept of trust repair in human-agent teams, demonstrating that calibration cues --- system-initiated signals about reliability --- can restore appropriate trust levels after trust violations, a mechanism with clear pedagogical applications.

The translation of these frameworks to educational contexts, however, demands careful attention to what makes educational AI distinctive. Unlike industrial or military automation, where the user's goal is task completion, educational AI operates in a domain where the "user" is simultaneously a *learner* whose cognitive development is the primary objective. This creates a paradox: the most helpful AI response (providing the correct answer) may be the most pedagogically harmful one (depriving the learner of productive struggle). Trust calibration in educational AI must therefore account not only for whether learners trust AI appropriately in terms of accuracy, but also for whether they understand the pedagogical implications of their reliance patterns.

### 2.2 The Calibration Core: Two Types of Calibration

At the heart of this paper lies the concept of *calibration* --- the correspondence between judgment and reality. In the trust and automation literature, Lee and See (2004) defined trust calibration as the degree of agreement between a person's trust in a system and the system's actual capabilities. Perfectly calibrated trust means that the user's confidence in the system neither exceeds nor falls short of what the system can reliably deliver. This concept provides a precise analytical lens for diagnosing two modes of trust failure.

*Overtrust* occurs when a learner's trust in an AI system exceeds the system's actual reliability. The behavioral consequence is uncritical acceptance: the learner treats AI outputs as authoritative without verification, suppressing the evaluative cognition that drives learning. The Bastani et al. (2025) findings provide a vivid empirical demonstration. Students with unrestricted ChatGPT access exhibited what the automation literature terms *automation complacency* (Parasuraman & Manzey, 2010) --- a state in which monitoring of automated outputs diminishes because of excessive trust. In educational terms, overtrust induces *cognitive offloading* (Risko & Gilbert, 2016), where learners delegate not just task execution but also the cognitive effort that would produce learning. This is particularly insidious with large language models, whose fluent and confident outputs mask variable reliability across different task domains and difficulty levels (Ji et al., 2023).

*Undertrust* occurs when a learner's trust falls below the system's actual capability. The behavioral consequence is underreliance: the learner rejects AI assistance even when it would be beneficial, forgoing scaffolded learning opportunities. Undertrust may arise from prior negative experiences, low technology self-efficacy, or broader skepticism toward AI (Hoff & Bashir, 2015). While undertrust has received less empirical attention in educational contexts than overtrust, it represents a significant concern as AI tools become more capable and the opportunity cost of non-adoption grows (Parasuraman & Riley, 1997).

**The critical distinction: SRL calibration versus trust calibration.** Existing educational research addresses calibration extensively --- but almost exclusively in the context of self-regulated learning. SRL calibration, as theorized by Winne and Hadwin (1998) and elaborated by subsequent researchers (Azevedo, 2005; Greene & Azevedo, 2007; Panadero, 2017), refers to the accuracy of learners' judgments about *their own* knowledge, skills, and performance. A well-calibrated learner accurately assesses what they know and what they do not, enabling adaptive study strategies and appropriate help-seeking. Decades of research have established that SRL calibration is a powerful predictor of academic achievement (Dunlosky & Rawson, 2012) and that metacognitive monitoring --- the process by which learners evaluate their own comprehension --- is the mechanism through which calibration operates (Nelson & Narens, 1990).

Trust calibration, as conceptualized in this paper, differs fundamentally from SRL calibration in its *locus of uncertainty*. Where SRL calibration asks, "How accurately do I judge my own capabilities?", trust calibration asks, "How accurately do I judge the AI system's capabilities?" The locus of the judgment shifts from *self* to an *external system*. This shift has profound implications for the calibration mechanisms required. SRL calibration can be improved through metacognitive training that helps learners become more accurate monitors of their own cognition (Schraw, 1998). Trust calibration, by contrast, requires knowledge of the AI system's reliability profile --- its strengths, limitations, failure modes, and the conditions under which its outputs should and should not be trusted --- a form of *AI literacy* (Long & Magerko, 2020) that goes beyond general self-regulation.

The distinction is not merely semantic. Consider a student who is highly metacognitively skilled --- she accurately recognizes that she struggles with integral calculus (high SRL calibration). When she turns to ChatGPT for assistance, however, she may grossly overestimate the AI's mathematical reasoning accuracy, accepting a confidently stated but subtly incorrect solution without verification (low trust calibration). Her SRL calibration does not protect her from trust miscalibration because the two involve different objects of judgment and different informational requirements. Existing SRL frameworks (Zimmerman, 2000; Winne & Hadwin, 1998; Panadero, 2017) provide robust theories for the first type of calibration; the second type --- trust calibration in AI-mediated learning --- remains largely unaddressed in educational research. This paper argues that trust calibration constitutes a distinct regulatory challenge that demands its own theoretical attention and design solutions.

### 2.3 The S-O-R Model and Its Limitations

Wang et al. (2025) recently proposed a Stimulus-Organism-Response (S-O-R) model for understanding learner interaction with AI-assisted learning environments, representing one of the most systematic attempts to theorize trust in this context. In their framework, the *Stimulus* component captures AI system characteristics that initiate the trust formation process --- including perceived intelligence, anthropomorphism, and output quality. The *Organism* component models the internal psychological processes through which learners form trust, encompassing cognitive appraisal (evaluating AI competence), affective response (emotional reactions to AI interaction), and existing mental models of AI capabilities. Critically, Wang et al. distinguish between trust as a psychological state and reliance as a behavioral outcome, avoiding the conflation that weakens many prior studies. The *Response* component captures the behavioral and learning consequences: the degree of reliance on or resistance to AI outputs, and the downstream effects on knowledge acquisition and skill development.

This model makes several valuable contributions. By positioning trust as a mediating variable between AI characteristics and learning outcomes, it provides a mechanistic account of how AI features translate into educational effects. By distinguishing trust formation from reliance behavior, it creates conceptual space for understanding cases where trust and reliance diverge --- for instance, when a learner trusts an AI system but deliberately chooses not to rely on it for pedagogical reasons, or when a learner distrusts an AI but relies on it due to time pressure. The model also acknowledges the role of self-calibration within the Organism construct, recognizing that learners' assessments of their own abilities interact with their evaluations of AI capabilities.

However, the S-O-R model has limitations that motivate the present framework. First, the model carries a *behaviorist lineage*. The Stimulus-Organism-Response framework originated in Woodworth's (1929) behaviorist psychology, and despite Wang et al.'s enrichment of the Organism construct, the model's input-process-output structure does not naturally accommodate the recursive, constructivist nature of learning (Piaget, 1971; Vygotsky, 1978). Learning is not a response to stimuli; it is an active process of knowledge construction in which the learner transforms both the material and themselves through engagement. In retaining the structural skeleton of the S-O-R model for its heuristic value, we extend the Organism construct to explicitly incorporate metacognitive self-regulation (Zimmerman, 2000), reflecting the constructivist imperative that educational scholarship demands.

Second, Wang et al.'s framework, as a single study contribution, would benefit from diversification through integration with the broader trust literature. The model's trust constructs can be enriched by incorporating Mayer et al.'s (1995) ABI dimensions (ability, benevolence, integrity), which provide a more granular vocabulary for the antecedents of trust, and Hoff and Bashir's (2015) three-layer model, which accounts for the dispositional and situational factors that shape initial trust states before any AI interaction occurs.

Third, and most consequentially for the present paper, the S-O-R model *stops at the individual learner level*. It models how trust forms and functions within the learner but does not address how educational systems can be designed to *actively calibrate* that trust. This is the gap between describing a problem and prescribing a solution. Knowing that overtrust harms learning (Bastani et al., 2025) is necessary but insufficient; what is needed is a framework that specifies how educational AI systems can detect trust miscalibration and intervene to correct it. The S-O-R model provides no mechanism for this system-level response, creating the specific theoretical gap that our Two-Level Framework aims to fill.

### 2.4 Toward a Two-Level Framework

The preceding analysis reveals a consistent pattern across existing theoretical approaches to trust in educational AI. Technology acceptance models (Davis, 1989; Venkatesh et al., 2003) address adoption but not ongoing trust calibration. SRL frameworks (Zimmerman, 2000; Winne & Hadwin, 1998) address self-calibration but not trust calibration with respect to external AI systems. The S-O-R model (Wang et al., 2025) addresses learner-level trust dynamics but not system-level design responses. Mayer et al.'s (1995) ABI model provides trust antecedents but no calibration mechanism. In each case, the theoretical contribution is valuable but incomplete. What is missing is a framework that connects the learner-level question --- "How does trust form, function, and miscalibrate?" --- to the design-level question --- "How can educational AI systems be built to calibrate trust?"

This paper proposes a Two-Level Trust Calibration Framework to address this gap. The framework operates at two interconnected levels:

**Level 1 (Micro): Learner Trust Dynamics.** This level models the internal cognitive and affective processes through which learners form, maintain, and adjust trust in AI systems. Drawing on Lee and See (2004), Hoff and Bashir (2015), and Mayer et al. (1995), it captures how trust is shaped by AI system characteristics, learner dispositions, situational factors, and accumulated experience. Critically, it models the interaction between trust calibration and SRL calibration, recognizing that both operate simultaneously during AI-mediated learning.

**Level 2 (Macro): System-Mediated Trust Calibration.** This level specifies how educational AI systems can be designed to detect and correct trust miscalibration through adaptive oversight. It introduces the concept of *Trust Calibration Scaffolds* --- system-level interventions that adjust in response to observed trust states to guide learners toward appropriate trust levels.

Connecting the two levels is the **Adaptive Calibration Cycle**, a four-stage process aligned with SRL theory that continuously monitors learner behavior, evaluates trust states, and adjusts scaffold intensity. This cycle serves as the operational engine of the framework, ensuring that trust calibration is not a one-time intervention but an ongoing, responsive process.

The connective tissue binding these levels is *scaffolding theory* (Wood, Bruner, & Ross, 1976), which provides both the pedagogical rationale and the design principles for Trust Calibration Scaffolds. Scaffolding, in its original formulation, refers to the temporary, adaptive support provided by a more knowledgeable other to help a learner accomplish tasks beyond their current independent capability. Three properties of scaffolding are particularly relevant. *Contingency* requires that support be calibrated to the learner's current state --- not fixed but dynamically adjusted based on ongoing assessment (van de Pol et al., 2010). *Fading* requires that support be gradually withdrawn as the learner's competence increases, preventing the dependence that undermines long-term learning. *Transfer of responsibility* captures the ultimate goal: shifting regulatory control from the external scaffold to the learner's own metacognitive resources. The empirical strength of scaffolding as an instructional strategy is well established; Hattie's (2009) synthesis reported an effect size of *d* = 0.82, placing it among the most potent instructional interventions identified in educational research. Hannafin et al. (1999) provided a taxonomy of scaffold types --- conceptual, metacognitive, procedural, and strategic --- that informs the categorization of Trust Calibration Scaffolds developed in Section 5.

Applied to trust calibration, scaffolding theory suggests that educational AI systems should provide contingent support that helps learners develop accurate trust judgments, fade that support as trust calibration improves, and ultimately transfer the responsibility for trust calibration from the system to the learner's own metacognitive apparatus. This application represents a novel extension of scaffolding theory from its traditional focus on cognitive task performance to the domain of trust regulation --- an extension that the Two-Level Framework formalizes and operationalizes.
## 3. Review Approach

This paper adopts a critical review approach (Grant & Booth, 2009) combined with conceptual framework development. Unlike a full systematic review or scoping review, which aim for exhaustive and reproducible literature coverage, a critical review prioritizes theoretical synthesis, critical appraisal of existing frameworks, and the identification of conceptual gaps that motivate new theoretical contributions (Pare et al., 2015). This methodological choice is deliberate and appropriate given the paper's primary contribution: not a comprehensive mapping of all trust-related studies in educational AI, but rather the development of the Two-Level Trust Calibration Framework --- a contribution that demands integrative theoretical reasoning and deep engagement with key literatures rather than exhaustive enumeration. Similar approaches have been adopted in recent high-impact educational technology scholarship for framework-oriented contributions (Williams, 2025), and are consistent with JBI guidelines that recognize conceptual framework development as a legitimate and valuable output of focused literature synthesis (Peters et al., 2020).

### Search Strategy

We conducted targeted literature searches across two major interdisciplinary databases: Web of Science and Scopus, covering the period from 2015 to 2026. This temporal scope was selected to capture the most consequential developments in AI-assisted learning, encompassing the maturation of intelligent tutoring systems, the emergence of learning analytics platforms, and the transformative period following the public release of large language models in late 2022. Search terms were constructed by combining trust-related terms --- ("trust" OR "trust calibration" OR "overtrust" OR "reliance" OR "overreliance") --- with educational AI terms --- ("educational AI" OR "AI-assisted learning" OR "generative AI" OR "intelligent tutoring" OR "AI in education" OR "learning analytics"). To ensure coverage of foundational theoretical works that predate the 2015 boundary, we supplemented database searches with backward and forward citation tracking from seminal works in the trust-in-automation tradition (Lee & See, 2004; Mayer et al., 1995; Hoff & Bashir, 2015), as well as recently published empirical and theoretical contributions that anchor the present framework (Wang et al., 2025; Bastani et al., 2025). This citation tracking was essential for tracing the intellectual lineage of trust constructs from human factors research into educational contexts.

### Selection Criteria

Papers were selected based on their relevance to trust dynamics in educational AI contexts, with priority given to three categories: (a) empirical studies directly measuring trust, reliance, or calibration in educational AI settings; (b) theoretical or conceptual frameworks addressing trust formation, trust calibration, or the trust-AI interaction in learning environments; and (c) studies from adjacent fields --- human factors, automation, cognitive psychology, and human-computer interaction --- that examine trust calibration or oversight mechanisms with clear applicability to education. Studies were excluded if they addressed trust only peripherally (e.g., as a single item in a technology acceptance survey without substantive analysis of trust dynamics) or if they focused exclusively on non-educational automation contexts without constructs transferable to learning settings. Through this process, approximately 40--60 key works were identified and subjected to in-depth analysis.

### Analysis Approach

Selected works were analyzed thematically, organized around four dimensions that correspond to the paper's research questions: (a) *trust conceptualization* --- how trust is defined, operationalized, and distinguished from related constructs such as acceptance, confidence, and reliance; (b) *theoretical frameworks* --- which theories guide trust research in educational AI, including technology acceptance models, SRL theories, organizational trust models, and human factors approaches; (c) *measurement approaches* --- the instruments and methods used to assess trust in educational AI contexts, with particular attention to the distinction between trust level and trust calibration accuracy; and (d) *calibration and oversight mechanisms* --- the extent to which existing work addresses the alignment between learner trust and AI capability, and whether system-level design responses to miscalibration are proposed. The fourth dimension served as the basis for our gap analysis, which specifically examined whether and how existing scholarship connects learner-level trust dynamics to system-level oversight design --- the connection that our Two-Level Framework aims to establish.

### Limitations

We acknowledge that this is not a full systematic or scoping review, and the search strategy, while targeted and rigorous, does not claim exhaustive coverage. Some relevant studies, particularly those published in non-English journals, disciplinary outlets outside our search scope, or as recent preprints, may have been missed. However, we contend that this limitation does not undermine the paper's central contribution. The primary value of this work lies in the Two-Level Trust Calibration Framework --- a theoretical contribution that requires deep engagement with key literatures and integrative synthesis rather than an exhaustive enumeration of all available studies. The critical review approach, with its emphasis on conceptual depth, gap identification, and framework development (Grant & Booth, 2009), is well suited to this purpose and is consistent with recognized review typologies that position critical reviews as appropriate vehicles for generating new theoretical insights from existing bodies of evidence.
## 4. Literature Landscape

The preceding sections established the methodological basis and theoretical foundations of this review. This section presents the findings from the literature analysis, organized around four dimensions: how trust is conceptualized, which theoretical frameworks predominate, how trust is measured, and where a critical gap emerges at the intersection of trust calibration and oversight design. Together, these dimensions reveal a field that has made substantial progress in documenting learner trust but has yet to address the more consequential question of how to calibrate it.

### 4.1 Trust Conceptualizations in Educational AI Research

Trust in educational AI research is not a unified construct. Across the reviewed literature, at least three distinct conceptualizations coexist, often without explicit acknowledgment of their differences. The first treats trust as an *attitude* toward the technology, following Lee and See's (2004) characterization of trust as "the attitude that an agent will help achieve an individual's goals in a situation characterized by uncertainty and vulnerability" (p. 54). The second treats trust as a *belief* about the trustee's characteristics, drawing on Mayer et al.'s (1995) widely cited model in which trust is a function of perceived ability, benevolence, and integrity. The third treats trust as a *behavioral intention*, operationalizing it through technology acceptance constructs such as willingness to use, perceived usefulness, and intention to adopt (Davis, 1989; Venkatesh et al., 2003).

In educational AI research specifically, the technology acceptance lens has been predominant [CONFIRM WITH LITERATURE]. The majority of studies examining learner trust in AI-powered tools operationalize trust through constructs derived from the Technology Acceptance Model (TAM) or the Unified Theory of Acceptance and Use of Technology (UTAUT), focusing on whether learners are willing to use AI rather than whether their willingness is epistemically warranted. This operationalization is consequential: it reduces trust to a binary or scalar variable (more trust is implicitly treated as better) and obscures the critical question of calibration accuracy.

A smaller body of work draws on the organizational trust tradition (Mayer et al., 1995), examining the antecedents of trust formation such as perceived competence of the AI system and institutional credibility. While this approach captures more nuance than TAM-based operationalizations, it still treats trust primarily as a dependent variable to be predicted rather than a dynamic state to be calibrated.

Notably absent from the educational AI trust literature is the calibration-oriented conceptualization that has gained traction in human factors and automation research. In that tradition, the central concern is not whether operators trust automation, but whether their trust *accurately tracks* the system's actual reliability across different contexts and conditions (Lee & See, 2004; Parasuraman & Riley, 1997). This distinction between trust level and trust accuracy is foundational to the framework proposed in Section 5, yet it remains underrepresented in educational AI scholarship [CONFIRM WITH LITERATURE].

### 4.2 Theoretical Frameworks in the Field

The theoretical frameworks employed in educational AI trust research cluster into several identifiable families, each carrying distinct assumptions about the nature of trust and its role in learning.

The dominant cluster comprises TAM and UTAUT extensions [CONFIRM WITH LITERATURE]. These frameworks treat trust as one among several predictors of technology adoption, positioning it alongside perceived ease of use, social influence, and facilitating conditions. While these models have generated substantial empirical work on the determinants of AI adoption in educational settings, they are structurally limited in their capacity to address trust dynamics: they model adoption as an outcome rather than as an ongoing process, and they do not account for the possibility that adoption itself may be harmful when trust is miscalibrated.

A second cluster draws on Mayer et al.'s (1995) integrative model of organizational trust, with its tripartite structure of ability, benevolence, and integrity (ABI). This framework has been adapted to AI contexts by mapping ability onto AI competence, benevolence onto perceived alignment of AI design with learner interests, and integrity onto consistency and transparency of AI behavior. The ABI model brings greater theoretical depth than TAM-based approaches but was originally developed for interpersonal trust relationships and requires careful adaptation when applied to human-AI interaction in learning contexts.

A third, emerging cluster applies trust-in-automation theory from human factors research (Lee & See, 2004; Hoff & Bashir, 2015) to educational AI. This tradition distinguishes among dispositional trust (stable individual tendencies), situational trust (context-dependent responses), and learned trust (accumulated through experience). Hoff and Bashir's (2015) three-layer model is particularly relevant because it accounts for the dynamic, evolving nature of trust that TAM-based models largely overlook. However, its adoption in educational AI research remains limited [CONFIRM WITH LITERATURE].

Additional frameworks identified include stimulus-organism-response (S-O-R) models adapted to AI-mediated learning (Wang et al., 2025), self-efficacy and self-regulated learning (SRL) frameworks (Bandura, 1997; Zimmerman, 2000), and social cognitive theory approaches. A critical observation across these clusters is that most frameworks in use were developed *for* general technology acceptance or organizational behavior and subsequently adapted *to* educational AI contexts, rather than being developed specifically for the unique conditions of AI-mediated learning, where the user's cognitive development is itself at stake. This distinction matters: in most technology acceptance scenarios, the user's primary goal is task completion; in educational AI use, the user's primary goal is learning, which may require precisely the kind of effortful processing that efficient AI use can eliminate.

### 4.3 Trust Measurement Approaches

The measurement landscape in educational AI trust research exhibits a pronounced methodological asymmetry. Self-report instruments dominate, behavioral measures are infrequent, and process measures are rare.

The prevailing approach relies on Likert-type trust questionnaires, typically administered as pre-post or cross-sectional surveys. These instruments capture learners' stated trust attitudes at discrete time points and have the advantage of psychometric familiarity and ease of administration. However, they are susceptible to well-documented limitations including social desirability bias, retrospective rationalization, and insensitivity to the dynamic, context-dependent fluctuations in trust that characterize real-time AI interaction [CONFIRM WITH LITERATURE].

Behavioral measures of trust, though more ecologically valid, appear in a comparatively small proportion of the reviewed studies [CONFIRM WITH LITERATURE]. Where they exist, they typically examine AI output acceptance rates (whether learners adopt AI-generated suggestions), usage frequency, or task delegation patterns. These measures offer more direct evidence of trust-in-action than self-reports but are rarely analyzed in relation to AI reliability, making it difficult to distinguish calibrated trust from uncritical reliance.

Process-level measures represent the most significant gap. Indicators such as time spent verifying AI outputs, patterns of modification and revision following AI suggestions, information-seeking behaviors triggered by AI recommendations, and the sequence and depth of learner engagement with AI-generated content are virtually absent from the reviewed literature [CONFIRM WITH LITERATURE]. This absence is consequential because process measures are precisely what would be needed to assess trust calibration in real time, a requirement for the adaptive framework proposed in Section 5.

Perhaps most critically, calibration-specific measurement, defined as the systematic assessment of alignment between a learner's perceived AI capability and the AI system's actual capability in a given domain, is essentially nonexistent in the educational AI trust literature. The field measures how much learners trust AI; it does not measure how *accurately* they trust it. This distinction is not merely semantic. A learner who reports high trust in an AI system that is, in fact, highly reliable for the given task is well-calibrated. A learner who reports equally high trust in a system that is unreliable for the given task is dangerously miscalibrated. Current measurement approaches cannot distinguish between these two states because they assess trust level without reference to the reliability baseline against which trust should be evaluated.

### 4.4 The Calibration Gap

The preceding subsections converge on a finding that constitutes the central empirical justification for the framework proposed in Section 5: despite growing research on trust in educational AI, a critical gap persists at the intersection of trust calibration and oversight design.

This gap manifests along two dimensions. First, studies that examine trust in educational AI rarely address how to intervene when trust is miscalibrated. The dominant research paradigm identifies antecedents (what predicts higher or lower trust), correlates (what trust is associated with), and outcomes (what trust levels lead to), but stops short of proposing mechanisms for *correcting* trust when it diverges from the warranted level. Trust is treated as a variable to be predicted, not a state to be managed.

Second, studies that examine AI scaffolding, oversight mechanisms, and pedagogical design in AI-mediated learning environments rarely incorporate trust as a mediating or moderating variable. The scaffolding literature addresses how to structure AI support for learning effectiveness but does not typically ask whether learners' trust in the scaffold is appropriate, excessive, or insufficient. The result is a design paradigm that optimizes AI scaffolding based on task characteristics and learning objectives without accounting for the learner's epistemic stance toward the AI system.

The disconnect between these two bodies of work can be stated concisely: trust researchers study what learners feel and believe about AI; design researchers study what AI systems should do for learners; almost no one systematically connects the two by asking how system design should respond to learner trust states and, reciprocally, how system design shapes those trust states over time.

Of the studies reviewed, an exceptionally small number explicitly address both trust calibration (not merely trust level) and oversight design (not merely system features) within an integrated framework [CONFIRM WITH LITERATURE]. This paucity is not a matter of insufficient research volume; it reflects a structural disciplinary divide between human factors research, where trust calibration is an established construct, and educational technology research, where scaffolding design is a mature tradition.

The empirical work of Bastani et al. (2025) exemplifies both the urgency of this gap and the cost of leaving it unaddressed. Their randomized controlled trial documented substantial learning deterioration among students who used AI tutoring, a finding attributable to overtrust and cognitive offloading. Crucially, however, their study documented the *harm* of miscalibrated trust without proposing *calibration mechanisms* to prevent or remediate it. The study demonstrates what happens when learners overtrust AI in educational settings; it does not address what systems or pedagogies could have detected and corrected the miscalibration before harm occurred.

This pattern, rigorous identification of trust-related problems without corresponding development of trust calibration solutions, characterizes the current state of the field. It creates a clear theoretical and practical need for frameworks that bridge learner-level trust dynamics with system-level design decisions, connecting the micro-processes of trust formation with the macro-structures of oversight and scaffolding. The Two-Level Trust Calibration Framework proposed in the following section is designed to address precisely this integration gap.
## 5. The Two-Level Trust Calibration Framework

This section presents the core contribution of this paper: a Two-Level Trust Calibration Framework that integrates learner-level trust dynamics (Level 1) with system-level oversight design (Level 2) through an adaptive calibration cycle. The framework is evidence-informed and conceptual; its novelty lies not in the individual components, each of which draws on established theory and evidence from related fields, but in their integration and application to the specific problem of trust calibration in educational AI environments.

### 5.1 From Fragmented Trust to Integrated Framework

As documented in Section 4, the current research landscape addresses trust dynamics and system design in isolation. Studies of learner trust describe how trust forms, changes, and influences behavior during AI interaction, but they do not prescribe how systems should respond when trust is miscalibrated. Conversely, studies of AI scaffolding and oversight design propose system architectures and pedagogical strategies but rarely incorporate the learner's trust state as a variable that shapes design decisions. No existing framework connects these two levels through a dynamic feedback mechanism that senses learner trust states and adjusts system behavior accordingly.

The Two-Level Trust Calibration Framework addresses this integration gap by drawing on four theoretical traditions. From trust-in-automation theory (Lee & See, 2004; Hoff & Bashir, 2015), it inherits the distinction between trust level and trust calibration, and the understanding that trust is a dynamic, multi-layered construct that must be evaluated against system reliability rather than treated as an unconditional good. From scaffolding theory (Wood et al., 1976), it inherits the principles of contingent support, fading, and transfer of responsibility --- principles that provide the pedagogical logic for adaptive oversight and that distinguish educationally grounded interventions from ad hoc design constraints. From self-regulated learning theory (Zimmerman, 2000; Winne & Hadwin, 1998), it inherits the cyclical model of performance, monitoring, evaluation, and adaptation that structures the calibration cycle and ensures that the framework captures the recursive, developmental nature of trust calibration. From adjacent field evidence on desirable difficulties (Bjork & Bjork, 2011), productive failure (Kapur, 2008, 2016), and trust repair in human-agent interaction (de Visser et al., 2020), it derives specific mechanisms that can be deployed as calibration scaffolds --- mechanisms with strong empirical grounding in their originating fields that await systematic application to the educational AI trust domain. The framework is thus integrative rather than additive: it does not simply combine existing theories but proposes a new architecture in which system-level design shapes the conditions under which learner-level calibration processes operate, and learner-level behavioral signals continuously inform system-level design adjustments.

### 5.2 Level 1 --- Learner Trust Dynamics (Micro)

Level 1 of the framework models the internal cognitive and metacognitive processes through which learners form, maintain, and revise trust judgments during interaction with AI systems. Its structural basis is adapted from Wang et al.'s (2025) integrated model of trust in AI-assisted learning, with a critical extension: the explicit incorporation of metacognitive processes as mediators of trust calibration, a dimension that Wang et al.'s Stimulus-Organism-Response architecture acknowledges but does not fully theorize.

**Trust antecedents.** AI system characteristics serve as the initial stimuli that shape trust formation. These include the transparency of AI reasoning processes, the observed accuracy of AI outputs, the design quality of the interface, and the consistency of AI behavior across interactions. These characteristics function as informational inputs from which learners construct mental models of AI reliability --- mental models that may be accurate or distorted depending on the learner's capacity for critical evaluation and the information the system makes available.

**Trust formation and metacognition.** At the core of Level 1 is the learner's trust state, conceptualized not as a static attitude but as a dynamic judgment that operates in conjunction with metacognitive processes. Drawing on Hoff and Bashir's (2015) three-layer model, trust at this level comprises three interacting layers: *dispositional trust*, reflecting stable individual differences in propensity to trust technology, shaped by personality, prior experience, and cultural context; *situational trust*, shaped by contextual factors including task difficulty, perceived stakes, time pressure, and immediate AI behavior; and *learned trust*, accumulated through direct experience with the AI system over time and subject to updating as interaction history accumulates. Critically, these trust layers do not operate in isolation from metacognition. The learner's capacity for self-monitoring, self-evaluation, and strategic regulation (Zimmerman, 2000) determines whether trust judgments are subjected to reflective scrutiny or accepted unreflectively --- a distinction that separates calibrated trust from the kind of uncritical reliance documented by Bastani et al. (2025).

**Internal calibration mechanisms.** Several learner-side processes contribute to the accuracy of trust judgments. Metacognitive monitoring enables learners to recognize discrepancies between their own understanding and AI-generated outputs, activating the evaluative processes that prevent automatic acceptance. Critical evaluation skills support the systematic assessment of AI output quality against domain knowledge and logical consistency. AI literacy, understood as knowledge of how AI systems function, their capabilities, and their characteristic failure modes (Long & Magerko, 2020), provides the domain-specific knowledge base necessary for informed trust judgments --- a learner who understands that large language models generate fluent text regardless of factual accuracy is better positioned to calibrate trust than one who equates fluency with reliability. Self-directed learning dispositions influence whether learners actively seek to verify and extend AI-provided information or passively consume it.

**Trust manifestation.** The behavioral output of the trust-metacognition interaction takes two primary forms: reliance (accepting, adopting, or building upon AI output) and resistance (rejecting, modifying, or disregarding AI output). Neither reliance nor resistance is inherently appropriate; their value depends on whether they are calibrated to the actual reliability of the AI system for the given task. Excessive reliance in the face of unreliable AI output constitutes overtrust --- the condition documented by Bastani et al. (2025) in which learner performance on unassisted tasks deteriorated following AI tutoring, as students bypassed the effortful cognitive processing that constitutes genuine learning. Excessive resistance in the face of reliable AI output constitutes undertrust, resulting in missed learning opportunities and inefficient allocation of cognitive resources (Parasuraman & Riley, 1997).

**Learning outcomes.** The downstream effects of calibrated or miscalibrated trust on knowledge construction, skill development, and transfer capacity constitute the ultimate dependent variable of Level 1. Overtrust degrades learning through cognitive offloading (Risko & Gilbert, 2016), reducing the effortful processing necessary for durable encoding and schema construction. Undertrust degrades learning through opportunity cost, denying learners access to potentially valuable scaffolding, feedback, and cognitive partnership.

A key limitation of Level 1 considered alone is that it is descriptive rather than prescriptive. It models what happens within the learner during AI interaction --- how trust forms, how it interacts with metacognition, and how it manifests in behavior --- but does not address how external systems should intervene when trust is miscalibrated. This limitation motivates Level 2.

### 5.3 Level 2 --- System-Mediated Trust Calibration (Macro)

Level 2 addresses the design question that Level 1 leaves unanswered: how should educational AI systems be structured to detect and correct miscalibrated trust? This level specifies six Trust Calibration Scaffolds, each grounded in established educational or psychological theory and each targeting a specific dimension of trust miscalibration. The term "scaffold" is used deliberately: these interventions are intended to be contingent (responsive to the learner's current trust state), fading (reduced as calibration improves), and responsibility-transferring (ultimately equipping learners to calibrate trust independently) --- the three defining properties of pedagogical scaffolding (Wood et al., 1976).

**Scaffold 1: Metacognitive Prompts.** Drawing on the metacognitive prompting literature (Schraw & Dennison, 1994; Bannert et al., 2009), this scaffold involves system-generated prompts that require learners to articulate, evaluate, or justify their own reasoning before, during, or after AI interaction. For example, a system might prompt learners to formulate their own hypothesis before viewing an AI-generated response, or to explain why they chose to accept or reject a particular AI suggestion. The mechanism targets overtrust by activating self-monitoring processes that might otherwise remain dormant during fluent AI interaction. Guo et al.'s (2022) meta-analysis of metacognitive scaffolding in technology-enhanced learning environments reported a moderate-to-strong aggregate effect (*g* = 0.50) on self-regulated learning outcomes and a meaningful effect (*g* = 0.40) on learning outcomes, providing robust evidence for the efficacy of this scaffold type in related contexts [CONFIRM WITH LITERATURE].

**Scaffold 2: Desirable Difficulties.** Grounded in Bjork and Bjork's (2011) desirable difficulties framework, this scaffold introduces deliberate challenges that impede immediate task performance but enhance long-term learning and trust calibration. In the context of AI-mediated learning, desirable difficulties may include requiring learners to produce initial solution attempts before AI assistance becomes available, introducing response delays that prevent reflexive AI reliance, or presenting AI outputs in formats that require active processing rather than passive consumption. The mechanism operates by restoring the effortful cognitive engagement that unconstrained AI access can eliminate. Importantly, the difficulties are "desirable" only insofar as they induce processing that strengthens durable learning; arbitrary or excessive difficulty serves no calibration function and risks producing frustration without pedagogical benefit.

**Scaffold 3: Productive Failure Points.** Based on Kapur's (2008, 2016) productive failure paradigm, this scaffold structures learning sequences so that learners first attempt problems without AI assistance, experience the boundaries of their own knowledge, and then receive AI support with explicit guidance on why and how the AI's contribution differs from or extends their own attempts. Productive failure has demonstrated effects up to twice those of conventional direct instruction in mathematics education (Kapur, 2016), and its mechanism --- generating and exploring incorrect or incomplete solutions before receiving instruction --- directly addresses both overtrust (by building awareness of one's own capabilities relative to AI) and undertrust (by demonstrating specific domains where AI assistance adds genuine value) [CONFIRM WITH LITERATURE].

**Scaffold 4: AI Uncertainty Transparency.** This scaffold involves the explicit communication of AI confidence levels, known limitations, and domain boundaries to learners. Drawing on the explainable AI (XAI) literature, AI uncertainty transparency enables learners to make informed trust judgments calibrated to the system's own assessment of its reliability, rather than relying on global impressions of AI competence that may not differentiate across task types or difficulty levels. Implementation may include confidence score visualizations, explicit domain limitation disclosures, or comparative accuracy information across task types. The goal is not to reduce trust indiscriminately but to provide the information necessary for appropriately differentiated trust --- higher trust in domains where the AI is demonstrably reliable and appropriately tempered trust in domains where it is not. This scaffold is grounded in Lee and See's (2004) foundational argument that appropriate trust requires information about system capabilities and limitations.

**Scaffold 5: Socratic Dialogue.** Rooted in one of the oldest and most enduring pedagogical traditions, Socratic dialogue as implemented in AI systems involves AI-initiated questioning that probes learner understanding rather than providing direct answers. When a learner requests AI assistance, the system responds with questions designed to activate the learner's own reasoning processes: "What do you think the answer might be?", "What evidence supports your current approach?", "Can you identify where your reasoning might be going wrong?" This scaffold shifts the fundamental interaction mode from answer-giving to thinking-prompting, disrupting the cognitive offloading dynamic that characterizes overtrust while maintaining the learner's engagement with AI as a productive intellectual partner. The effectiveness of questioning over telling in promoting deeper learning is well established in the tutoring literature (Chi et al., 2001), though its specific application to trust calibration in AI-mediated contexts remains to be empirically tested.

**Scaffold 6: Progressive Autonomy Release.** Adapted from Wood et al.'s (1976) fading principle and elaborated by Pea (2004), this scaffold implements the gradual reduction of scaffold intensity as evidence of improved trust calibration accumulates. Progressive autonomy release embodies the core property of pedagogical scaffolding: support is inherently temporary, provided at maximum intensity when the learner needs it and systematically withdrawn as the learner develops the capacity for self-regulated performance. In the trust calibration context, this means that learners who demonstrate calibrated trust --- appropriately differentiating their reliance on AI across reliability contexts --- progressively receive less external prompting, fewer imposed difficulties, and greater autonomy in their AI interactions. Without fading, scaffolding risks creating dependence on the scaffold itself rather than developing the learner's internal calibration capacity.

Two important qualifications apply to Level 2 as a whole. First, adjacent field evidence substantially strengthens the theoretical grounding of these scaffolds. De Visser et al.'s (2020) work on trust repair in human-robot interaction demonstrates that calibration interventions can effectively restore appropriate trust following automation failures, providing a conceptual bridge to the educational AI context. However, direct empirical evidence for these specific scaffolds deployed specifically for trust calibration in educational AI is sparse. The framework is evidence-informed in the sense that its components draw on well-established theory and evidence from related fields, but it is conceptual in the sense that the integrated application to educational AI trust calibration has not yet been empirically tested. This honest positioning as an evidence-informed conceptual framework, consistent with the legitimate purposes of critical reviews as articulated by Grant and Booth (2009) and Peters et al. (2020), is essential to the framework's scholarly integrity. Second, the six scaffolds are not intended as an exhaustive taxonomy; they represent the mechanisms for which the strongest theoretical and empirical grounding can currently be identified. Future research may identify additional scaffold types as the evidence base for trust calibration in educational AI develops.

### 5.4 The Adaptive Calibration Cycle

The Adaptive Calibration Cycle constitutes the central engine of the framework, connecting the descriptive model of Level 1 with the prescriptive design of Level 2 through a continuous, dynamic feedback process. Aligned with the cyclical structure of self-regulated learning theory (Zimmerman, 2000), the cycle operates through four stages that repeat iteratively throughout the learning interaction.

**Stage 1 --- PERFORMANCE.** The learner interacts with the AI system, generating observable behavioral data. These behaviors --- including AI output acceptance or rejection decisions, the extent and nature of modifications made to AI suggestions, the time invested in examining and verifying AI outputs, information-seeking actions triggered by AI recommendations, and patterns of AI use across different task types and difficulty levels --- constitute the empirical basis from which trust states can be inferred. The performance stage does not require the learner to explicitly report their trust; rather, trust is indexed through its behavioral manifestations, an approach consistent with the behavioral indicators of trust established in the automation literature (Lee & See, 2004; Dzindolet et al., 2003).

**Stage 2 --- MONITORING.** The system detects patterns in behavioral data that signal the learner's current trust state. This stage is functionally analogous to the self-monitoring phase in SRL theory, but with a critical distinction: the monitoring is performed *by the system* about *the learner*, rather than by the learner about themselves. Monitoring metrics include the ratio of AI output acceptance to rejection, the proportion of AI suggestions adopted without modification, the average time spent examining AI outputs before acting on them, the frequency and depth of verification behaviors, and the consistency of these patterns across task types and AI reliability levels. The system's monitoring function supplements, rather than replaces, the learner's own metacognitive monitoring; both forms of monitoring are necessary for effective calibration, and the framework is designed to progressively transfer monitoring responsibility from the system to the learner as calibration improves.

**Stage 3 --- EVALUATION.** Based on monitored behavioral patterns, the system (or, in less automated implementations, the educator) evaluates the learner's trust state along three categories: overtrust, undertrust, or calibrated. The evaluation is inherently relational, requiring comparison of the learner's trust-indexed behaviors against the AI system's actual reliability profile for the relevant task domain. A learner who consistently accepts AI outputs without verification is not necessarily overtrusting; if the AI is genuinely reliable for those tasks, the behavior may reflect calibrated efficiency rather than complacency. The same behavior directed at unreliable AI outputs, however, constitutes overtrust. This context-sensitivity is essential and distinguishes trust calibration assessment from simple behavioral frequency counting. The Trust-Reliability Matrix introduced below provides the evaluative framework for this stage.

**Stage 4 --- ADAPTATION.** Based on the evaluation, scaffold intensity is adjusted through one of three responses. If overtrust is detected, scaffolds are intensified: desirable difficulties are introduced or increased, metacognitive prompts are activated, Socratic dialogue replaces direct answers, and productive failure opportunities are structured into the learning sequence. If undertrust is detected, transparency scaffolds are deployed: AI confidence information is made visible, guided comparison tasks demonstrate AI reliability in specific domains, and productive failure experiences are designed to highlight domains where AI assistance adds genuine value. If calibrated trust is detected, progressive autonomy release begins: scaffold intensity is gradually reduced, the learner is granted greater autonomy in AI interactions, and the system transitions toward a monitoring-only mode that can re-engage scaffolds if calibration deteriorates.

The four stages are formalized in a Dynamic Scaffolding Principle: as calibration improves, oversight decreases through scaffold fading and expanded learner autonomy; as calibration deteriorates, oversight increases through scaffold intensification and reintroduction of structured support. This bidirectional responsiveness distinguishes the framework from static oversight designs that apply uniform constraints regardless of learner state --- a one-size-fits-all approach that is either too intrusive for well-calibrated learners or insufficiently protective for miscalibrated ones.

**Figure 2** presents the Two-Level Framework visually, depicting Level 2 (System-Mediated Trust Calibration) in the upper register, Level 1 (Learner Trust Dynamics) in the lower register, and the Adaptive Calibration Cycle as a central band connecting the two. Bidirectional arrows indicate the sensing pathway (behavioral data flowing upward from Level 1 to inform the cycle's monitoring and evaluation stages) and the adjusting pathway (scaffold modifications flowing downward from the cycle to alter the learner's interaction conditions). This visual architecture makes explicit the framework's central claim: that effective trust calibration requires continuous, bidirectional communication between the learner's internal trust dynamics and the system's external oversight design.

**Figure 3** introduces the Trust-Reliability Calibration Matrix, a 2 x 2 analytic tool mapping learner trust (high vs. low) against AI reliability (high vs. low) to produce four distinct calibration states, each with different implications for learning and different scaffold responses.

*Quadrant I: High Trust + High AI Reliability = Calibrated Appropriate Use.* Trust is warranted because the AI is genuinely reliable in the given domain. This represents the target state and the condition under which progressive autonomy release (fading) is appropriate. Continued heavy scaffolding in this quadrant would be paternalistic and counterproductive to the development of self-regulated AI use.

*Quadrant II: High Trust + Low AI Reliability = Overtrust ("Bastani Trap").* This is the most dangerous miscalibration state. The learner trusts AI outputs that are unreliable for the purpose that matters most --- durable learning --- resulting in cognitive offloading, reduced effortful processing, and consequent skill atrophy. The learning deterioration observed by Bastani et al. (2025) represents precisely this quadrant: high learner trust paired with functionally unreliable AI outputs for novel problem types requiring independent reasoning, with no calibration mechanism to correct the misalignment. The prescribed scaffold response is intensification --- deployment of desirable difficulties, productive failure tasks, and metacognitive prompts to restore the effortful processing that overtrust eliminates.

*Quadrant III: Low Trust + High AI Reliability = Undertrust / Missed Benefit.* The learner's skepticism, while epistemically virtuous in Quadrant II, is maladaptive here. The AI system is genuinely reliable, but the learner does not benefit from it because trust is insufficient to support productive engagement. Prescribed scaffold responses include AI uncertainty transparency (making reliability evidence visible), guided comparison tasks that allow learners to verify AI accuracy directly, and productive failure experiences designed to demonstrate domains where AI assistance genuinely outperforms unaided performance.

*Quadrant IV: Low Trust + Low AI Reliability = Calibrated Avoidance.* The learner's low trust accurately reflects low AI reliability, representing appropriate skepticism rather than a failure of calibration. The scaffold response is maintenance: reinforce the verification habits and metacognitive processes that produce accurate calibration, build AI literacy to support ongoing discrimination between reliable and unreliable AI contexts, and monitor for potential drift toward overtrust as AI systems improve or as the novelty of appropriate skepticism fades.

**The Initialization Principle.** A potential concern with the Adaptive Calibration Cycle is circularity: the system must know the learner's calibration state to select appropriate scaffolds, but the calibration state is itself shaped by scaffolding. This concern is addressed through an explicit initialization principle. When the learner's calibration state is unknown --- as at the beginning of any new learning interaction --- the system defaults to high oversight, deploying scaffolds at maximum intensity. As behavioral data accumulates through the performance and monitoring stages, the system progressively refines its model of the learner's trust state and adjusts scaffold intensity accordingly. This initialization logic mirrors the foundational scaffolding principle of providing maximum support initially and fading as learner competence develops (Wood et al., 1976). The apparent circularity is thus reframed as a design feature: the system operates adaptively under uncertainty, converging on appropriate calibration through iterative observation and adjustment rather than requiring a priori knowledge of learner states.

## 6. Implications

The Two-Level Trust Calibration Framework carries implications that extend across three primary audiences: AI system designers, educators, and researchers. Rather than treating these implications as general recommendations, this section derives specific, actionable principles from the framework's architecture and its grounding in educational theory.

### 6.1 Implications for AI System Designers

The framework's integration of learner-level trust dynamics with system-level oversight architecture yields five design principles that challenge prevailing assumptions in educational AI development.

**Principle 1: Design for Calibration, Not Just Trust.** The dominant design logic in educational AI treats learner trust as a success metric --- higher trust is assumed to indicate a better system. The framework proposed here reframes trust as a calibration target rather than a maximization objective. AI systems should aim not to increase trust per se but to align trust with actual system capability for specific tasks. A system that is trusted exactly as much as it deserves is safer and more educationally productive than one that is universally trusted. This means that under certain conditions --- notably, when AI reliability is low for a given domain or task type --- the design goal should be to *reduce* learner trust to a level commensurate with actual capability. Designers should specify calibration targets alongside performance metrics in system requirements, drawing on the Trust-AI Reliability Matrix (Figure 3) to define what appropriate trust looks like for different reliability contexts.

**Principle 2: Embed Metacognitive Scaffolds by Default.** Every AI-assisted learning interaction should include prompts that activate learner reflection on their reliance decisions. The evidence base for metacognitive prompting is substantial: Guo et al. (2022) reported a medium effect size (*g* = 0.50) for self-regulated learning outcomes in their meta-analysis of technology-enhanced learning environments. Default prompts such as "What do you think the answer is before seeing the AI's response?" or "How confident are you in the AI's suggestion, and why?" should be architectural features rather than optional additions. The cost of implementation is minimal; the cost of omission, as Bastani et al. (2025) demonstrated, is measurable learning degradation. Bannert et al. (2015) further showed that such prompts can produce both short- and long-term effects on problem-solving performance, suggesting that the benefits of embedded metacognitive scaffolds compound over time rather than diminishing.

**Principle 3: Implement Transparency as Standard Practice.** AI systems deployed in educational contexts should display confidence levels, known limitations, and uncertainty indicators as standard interface elements. Learners cannot calibrate trust without information about system reliability (Lee & See, 2004). This principle draws on the explainable AI literature, which has demonstrated that appropriate transparency can reduce overtrust in high-capability systems and build warranted trust in contexts where the system performs well (Arrieta et al., 2020; Gunning et al., 2019). Transparency should be calibrated to learner expertise: novice learners may require simplified reliability indicators, while advanced learners can benefit from more granular uncertainty information. Amershi et al. (2019) articulated guidelines for human-AI interaction that reinforce this principle, emphasizing that users should be able to understand what AI systems can and cannot do.

**Principle 4: Design Adaptive Oversight.** The Adaptive Calibration Cycle at the core of the framework implies that scaffold intensity should not be static. Instead, oversight should adapt to the learner's current trust calibration state. The cycle provides the operational mechanism: monitor behavioral signals, evaluate calibration state, and adapt scaffold intensity accordingly. The initialization principle follows from scaffolding theory (Wood et al., 1976): begin with maximum scaffolding intensity --- frequent metacognitive prompts, mandatory verification steps, visible uncertainty displays --- and fade these supports as evidence of calibrated trust accumulates. Adaptive oversight requires behavioral sensing mechanisms that detect calibration signals such as verification frequency, AI acceptance rates, and modification patterns. Systems that lack such sensing mechanisms default to one-size-fits-all scaffolding, which is either too intrusive for well-calibrated learners or insufficiently protective for miscalibrated ones.

**Principle 5: Plan for Progressive Autonomy Release.** The ultimate design goal is learner independence from external scaffolding --- the fading principle articulated by Wood et al. (1976) and supported by Hattie's (2009) meta-analytic evidence on scaffolding effectiveness (*d* = 0.82). Educational AI systems should be designed with explicit fading mechanisms that progressively transfer calibration responsibility from the system to the learner. This means building in assessment points where the system evaluates whether the learner can maintain calibrated trust without prompting, and designing graceful transitions from scaffolded to autonomous AI use. The Dynamic Scaffolding Principle (Calibration up leads to Oversight down) should be architecturally embedded, not treated as an afterthought. Systems without fading mechanisms risk creating a different form of dependency: reliance on the scaffold itself rather than on the learner's own metacognitive resources.

### 6.2 Implications for Educators

The framework positions educators not merely as content deliverers who happen to use AI tools, but as active agents in the trust calibration process. This reframing carries several practical implications.

First, trust calibration should be recognized as a distinct learning objective, one that sits alongside content mastery and generic metacognitive skill development. The capacity to accurately assess an AI system's reliability for a given task, and to adjust one's reliance accordingly, is an epistemic competence that must be explicitly taught, practiced, and assessed (Long & Magerko, 2020; Ng et al., 2021). It is not a byproduct of AI exposure but a skill that requires intentional cultivation. Just as educators teach students to evaluate the credibility of human-authored sources, they must now teach students to evaluate the reliability of AI-generated outputs --- a qualitatively different challenge given AI's capacity to produce fluent but incorrect content.

Second, educators serve as trust calibration agents whose role extends beyond content delivery to include trust monitoring and intervention. Using the Trust-AI Reliability Matrix (Figure 3) as a diagnostic tool, educators can identify whether individual learners or classroom cohorts are operating in calibrated states (Quadrants I or IV) or miscalibrated states (Quadrants II or III) and deploy targeted interventions accordingly. For overtrusting learners, this may involve AI evaluation exercises in which students assess AI output quality against expert standards, thereby building the evaluative dispositions that prevent uncritical acceptance. For undertrusting learners, guided comparison tasks demonstrating AI reliability in appropriate contexts can build warranted trust without encouraging overreliance.

Third, AI literacy should be treated as a prerequisite for calibrated trust, not as a supplementary topic. Learners who lack understanding of how AI systems generate outputs, where their capabilities end, and how their performance varies across domains cannot engage in meaningful trust calibration (Long & Magerko, 2020; Ng et al., 2021). Educators should integrate AI literacy instruction before or alongside AI tool deployment, rather than assuming that familiarity will develop through use alone. This includes teaching students about the probabilistic nature of large language model outputs, their susceptibility to hallucination, and the domain-dependent variability of their accuracy.

Fourth, developmental readiness must inform scaffold design. Younger learners and those with limited prior AI experience require more intensive scaffolding and longer fading timelines (Vygotsky, 1978). The framework's adaptive mechanism accommodates this variation, but educators must exercise professional judgment in calibrating the pace of autonomy release to the specific learner population. Self-efficacy research (Bandura, 1997) suggests that learners with lower technology self-efficacy may need additional encouragement and guided success experiences before progressing toward autonomous AI use.

Finally, cultural factors in technology trust dispositions warrant attention. Research on cross-cultural variation in technology acceptance and trust (Li et al., 2008; Hofstede, 2001) suggests that learners from high power-distance cultures may exhibit greater initial deference to AI systems, creating elevated overtrust risk. Educators working in culturally diverse settings should attend to these dispositional differences when monitoring trust calibration states, modeling healthy skepticism while avoiding technophobia. The goal is not to produce uniform distrust but to cultivate appropriately differentiated trust that reflects genuine understanding of AI capabilities and limitations.

### 6.3 Implications for Researchers

The Two-Level Trust Calibration Framework generates a focused research agenda organized around five priority questions.

**1. Measurement Development.** How can trust calibration accuracy be reliably measured in educational contexts? The calibration gap identified in Section 4 is partly a measurement gap: the field lacks validated instruments that capture the alignment between learner trust and AI capability. Developing such instruments requires moving beyond self-report trust scales to multimethod approaches that integrate behavioral indicators (verification frequency, AI override rates, modification depth), process measures (time allocation patterns, information-seeking sequences), and self-report judgments anchored to specific AI reliability benchmarks. The calibration score methodology used in SRL research --- comparing confidence judgments to actual performance (Schraw & Dennison, 1994; Dunlosky & Rawson, 2012) --- offers a template that can be adapted for trust calibration by comparing trust judgments to AI accuracy metrics.

**2. Scaffold Effectiveness.** Which trust calibration scaffolds are most effective for which types of miscalibration, learner populations, and educational contexts? The framework identifies six scaffold categories, but the relative effectiveness of each, alone and in combination, remains an open empirical question. Experimental studies comparing scaffold types across different quadrants of the Trust-AI Reliability Matrix would provide the evidence needed to move from principled recommendation to evidence-based prescription. Of particular interest is whether scaffolds effective for overtrust correction (e.g., desirable difficulties; Bjork & Bjork, 2011) are equally effective across content domains and learner expertise levels, and whether productive failure interventions (Kapur, 2016; Loibl et al., 2017) transfer from mathematics education to AI-mediated learning contexts.

**3. Developmental Trajectory.** How does trust calibration develop over time? What accelerates or impedes its development? Longitudinal designs are needed to map the typical trajectory from initial AI interaction to calibrated trust, identifying critical transition points, common stalling patterns, and factors that accelerate or impede calibration development. De Visser et al.'s (2020) work on longitudinal trust calibration in human-robot teams provides methodological precedent, but educational contexts introduce unique temporal dynamics --- learning is inherently developmental, and trust calibration must be studied within that developmental frame. Ryan and Deci's (2000) self-determination theory may offer additional explanatory power regarding how autonomy, competence, and relatedness needs interact with trust calibration trajectories.

**4. Cultural Variation.** How do cultural factors moderate trust calibration processes and scaffold effectiveness? Cross-cultural research on technology trust (Li et al., 2008; Hofstede, 2001) and educational traditions suggests that the calibration dynamics described in the framework may operate differently across cultural contexts characterized by varying levels of power distance, uncertainty avoidance, and institutional trust in technology. Given the global deployment of educational AI systems (UNESCO, 2023), culturally sensitive adaptations of trust calibration scaffolds may be necessary, and the assumption that scaffolds developed in Western educational contexts will transfer without modification requires empirical scrutiny.

**5. Framework Validation.** Does the Two-Level Framework accurately predict trust calibration outcomes? The framework is a conceptual contribution that requires empirical testing through multiple methodological approaches. Design-based research methodologies (Anderson & Shattuck, 2012; McKenney & Reeves, 2019) are well suited to iterative framework refinement, while randomized controlled trials can test specific causal claims --- for example, whether adaptive scaffold fading produces superior calibration outcomes compared to static scaffolding. Moving from conceptual framework to evidence-based model is the essential next step in this research program, and the five design principles articulated in Section 6.1 provide testable propositions to anchor this empirical work.
## 7. Conclusion

### 7.1 Limitations

Several limitations of this work warrant explicit acknowledgment. First, regarding review scope, this paper adopted a critical review approach (Grant & Booth, 2009) rather than a full systematic review or scoping review following established protocols such as PRISMA-ScR (Peters et al., 2020). The critical review methodology was chosen deliberately to enable deeper theoretical synthesis and conceptual contribution, but this choice entails a trade-off: the literature search, while systematic in its use of defined databases, date ranges, and search terms, was not exhaustive. Relevant studies that a comprehensive, protocol-driven search would capture may have been missed, and the selection of literature for detailed analysis was guided by theoretical relevance rather than by predetermined inclusion algorithms. A full systematic or scoping review would provide more complete coverage and greater reproducibility of the literature mapping.

Second, the literature selection and analysis were conducted by a single researcher, introducing potential selection and interpretation bias. The thematic categorizations reported in Section 4 and the gap analysis that motivates the framework reflect a single analytical perspective. Future work should involve multiple independent reviewers to establish intercoder reliability for the classification of trust conceptualizations, theoretical frameworks, and measurement approaches, thereby strengthening the evidentiary foundation upon which the calibration gap claim rests.

Third, the search was limited to English-language publications, a constraint that may systematically exclude relevant work published in other languages. This limitation is particularly consequential given the global deployment of educational AI systems (UNESCO, 2023) and the likelihood that trust dynamics are shaped by cultural and linguistic contexts. Research traditions in East Asian, European, and other non-Anglophone contexts may offer perspectives on trust calibration that the present review could not capture. Given evidence that cultural dimensions such as power distance and uncertainty avoidance influence technology trust (Hofstede, 2001; Li et al., 2008), this linguistic limitation may have obscured culturally specific calibration dynamics.

Fourth, the Two-Level Trust Calibration Framework is a conceptual contribution that requires empirical validation. While the evidence mapping presented in Section 5 (Table 2) draws on established findings from adjacent fields --- metacognitive prompting (Guo et al., 2022), desirable difficulties (Bjork & Bjork, 2011), productive failure (Kapur, 2008, 2016), and scaffolding (Hattie, 2009; Wood et al., 1976) --- direct empirical evidence for trust calibration scaffolds operating as theorized in educational AI contexts remains sparse. The framework's predictions are theoretically grounded but empirically untested in integrated form. The gap between evidence-informed conceptual architecture and empirically validated prescriptive model must be bridged through the design-based research and experimental studies outlined in Section 6.3.

Fifth, the framework assumes relatively stable AI capability within defined contexts, enabling the Trust-AI Reliability Matrix to classify trust states based on a fixed reliability assessment. However, generative AI systems such as large language models exhibit variable and often unpredictable performance across tasks, domains, and even individual queries. This variability means that the Trust-AI Reliability Matrix requires dynamic rather than static assessment of AI reliability --- a requirement that adds implementation complexity and that the current framework acknowledges but does not fully resolve. As AI systems continue to evolve rapidly, the reliability landscape against which trust must be calibrated is itself a moving target, a challenge that future iterations of the framework must address more directly.

### 7.2 Conclusion

This paper has advanced a core argument: trust calibration --- the alignment between learner trust in AI and the AI system's actual capability for a given task --- represents the missing link in educational AI design. Through a critical review of the literature on trust in educational AI (2015--2026), we identified a structural gap at the intersection of two mature but disconnected research traditions: trust research in human factors and automation, which has long recognized calibration as the central concern (Lee & See, 2004; Parasuraman & Riley, 1997), and scaffolding research in educational technology, which has developed sophisticated approaches to adaptive learner support (Wood et al., 1976; Belland, 2014) but has not systematically applied them to the problem of trust.

The paper makes three interrelated contributions. First, it identifies and documents the calibration gap in the educational AI literature --- the finding that existing research predominantly treats trust as a unidimensional variable to be maximized rather than as a calibration target to be aligned with system capability. This gap is not peripheral; it is a structural absence that, if left unaddressed, will produce the overtrust effects documented by Bastani et al. (2025) at increasing scale as AI adoption in education accelerates globally.

Second, the paper proposes the Two-Level Trust Calibration Framework, which connects learner-level trust dynamics (Level 1) with system-level trust calibration mechanisms (Level 2) through an Adaptive Calibration Cycle aligned with self-regulated learning theory (Zimmerman, 2000; Winne & Hadwin, 1998). At the system level, the framework specifies six trust calibration scaffolds --- metacognitive prompts, desirable difficulties, productive failure, AI uncertainty transparency, Socratic dialogue, and progressive autonomy release --- each grounded in established educational theory and mapped to specific miscalibration states. The framework's adaptive architecture, in which scaffold intensity is continuously adjusted based on behavioral signals of calibration quality, provides a principled alternative to both unrestricted AI access and rigid usage constraints.

Third, the paper introduces conceptual tools for advancing both research and practice: the distinction between SRL calibration (accuracy of self-assessment) and trust calibration (accuracy of AI assessment), the Trust-AI Reliability Matrix for diagnostic assessment of learner trust states, and five design principles for trust-calibrated educational AI. These tools provide a shared vocabulary and analytic structure for a research community that has, to date, lacked the conceptual infrastructure to address trust calibration systematically.

The Bastani et al. (2025) finding --- that unrestricted AI access can harm learning through overtrust and cognitive offloading --- is not an anomaly. It is a predictable consequence of deploying capable AI systems in educational contexts without mechanisms for detecting and correcting trust miscalibration. The framework proposed here offers a principled, theoretically grounded approach to preventing such outcomes by treating trust calibration as a first-class design target rather than an afterthought.

The path forward is empirical. The conceptual architecture presented in this paper must be tested through design-based research (Anderson & Shattuck, 2012; McKenney & Reeves, 2019) that implements trust calibration scaffolds in authentic educational settings, measures their effects on calibration accuracy and learning outcomes, and iteratively refines the framework based on evidence. The research agenda outlined in Section 6.3 provides specific, testable propositions to guide this work.

As educational AI becomes increasingly autonomous and pervasive, the question for researchers and designers is no longer simply "Do learners trust AI?" but rather "Is their trust calibrated?" --- and critically, "Who or what calibrates it?" The answer to this question will determine whether the growing integration of AI in education produces learners who think critically with AI or learners who defer uncritically to it. Trust calibration is not an obstacle to AI adoption in education; it is the condition that makes AI adoption educationally defensible.
