## 7. Conclusion

### 7.1 Limitations

Several limitations of this work warrant explicit acknowledgment. First, regarding review scope, this paper adopted a critical review approach (Grant & Booth, 2009) rather than a full systematic review or scoping review following established protocols such as PRISMA-ScR (Peters et al., 2020). The critical review methodology was chosen deliberately to enable deeper theoretical synthesis and conceptual contribution, but this choice entails a trade-off: the literature search, while systematic in its use of defined databases, date ranges, and search terms, was not exhaustive. Relevant studies that a comprehensive, protocol-driven search would capture may have been missed, and the selection of literature for detailed analysis was guided by theoretical relevance rather than by predetermined inclusion algorithms. A full systematic or scoping review would provide more complete coverage and greater reproducibility of the literature mapping.

Second, the literature selection and analysis were conducted by a single researcher, introducing potential selection and interpretation bias. The thematic categorizations reported in Section 4 and the gap analysis that motivates the framework reflect a single analytical perspective. Future work should involve multiple independent reviewers to establish intercoder reliability for the classification of trust conceptualizations, theoretical frameworks, and measurement approaches, thereby strengthening the evidentiary foundation upon which the calibration gap claim rests.

Third, the search was limited to English-language publications, a constraint that may systematically exclude relevant work published in other languages. This limitation is particularly consequential given the global deployment of educational AI systems (UNESCO, 2023) and the likelihood that trust dynamics are shaped by cultural and linguistic contexts. Research traditions in East Asian, European, and other non-Anglophone contexts may offer perspectives on trust calibration that the present review could not capture. Given evidence that cultural dimensions such as power distance and uncertainty avoidance influence technology trust (Hofstede, 2001; Li et al., 2008), this linguistic limitation may have obscured culturally specific calibration dynamics.

Fourth, the Two-Level Trust Calibration Framework is a conceptual contribution that requires empirical validation. While the evidence mapping presented in Section 5 (Table 2) draws on established findings from adjacent fields --- metacognitive prompting (Guo et al., 2022), desirable difficulties (Bjork & Bjork, 2011), productive failure (Kapur, 2008, 2016), and scaffolding (Hattie, 2009; Wood et al., 1976) --- direct empirical evidence for trust calibration scaffolds operating as theorized in educational AI contexts remains sparse. The framework's predictions are theoretically grounded but empirically untested in integrated form. The gap between evidence-informed conceptual architecture and empirically validated prescriptive model must be bridged through the design-based research and experimental studies outlined in Section 6.3.

Fifth, the framework assumes relatively stable AI capability within defined contexts, enabling the Trust-AI Reliability Matrix to classify trust states based on a fixed reliability assessment. However, generative AI systems such as large language models exhibit variable and often unpredictable performance across tasks, domains, and even individual queries. This variability means that the Trust-AI Reliability Matrix requires dynamic rather than static assessment of AI reliability --- a requirement that adds implementation complexity and that the current framework acknowledges but does not fully resolve. As AI systems continue to evolve rapidly, the reliability landscape against which trust must be calibrated is itself a moving target, a challenge that future iterations of the framework must address more directly.

### 7.2 Conclusion

This paper has advanced a core argument: trust calibration --- the alignment between learner trust in AI and the AI system's actual capability for a given task --- represents the missing link in educational AI design. Through a critical review of the literature on trust in educational AI (2015--2026), we identified a structural gap at the intersection of two mature but disconnected research traditions: trust research in human factors and automation, which has long recognized calibration as the central concern (Lee & See, 2004; Parasuraman & Riley, 1997), and scaffolding research in educational technology, which has developed sophisticated approaches to adaptive learner support (Wood et al., 1976; Belland, 2014) but has not systematically applied them to the problem of trust.

The paper makes three interrelated contributions. First, it identifies and documents the calibration gap in the educational AI literature --- the finding that existing research predominantly treats trust as a unidimensional variable to be maximized rather than as a calibration target to be aligned with system capability. This gap is not peripheral; it is a structural absence that, if left unaddressed, will produce the overtrust effects documented by Bastani et al. (2025) at increasing scale as AI adoption in education accelerates globally.

Second, the paper proposes the Two-Level Trust Calibration Framework, which connects learner-level trust dynamics (Level 1) with system-level trust calibration mechanisms (Level 2) through an Adaptive Calibration Cycle aligned with self-regulated learning theory (Zimmerman, 2000; Winne & Hadwin, 1998). At the system level, the framework specifies six trust calibration scaffolds --- metacognitive prompts, desirable difficulties, productive failure, AI uncertainty transparency, Socratic dialogue, and progressive autonomy release --- each grounded in established educational theory and mapped to specific miscalibration states. The framework's adaptive architecture, in which scaffold intensity is continuously adjusted based on behavioral signals of calibration quality, provides a principled alternative to both unrestricted AI access and rigid usage constraints.

Third, the paper introduces conceptual tools for advancing both research and practice: the distinction between SRL calibration (accuracy of self-assessment) and trust calibration (accuracy of AI assessment), the Trust-AI Reliability Matrix for diagnostic assessment of learner trust states, and five design principles for trust-calibrated educational AI. These tools provide a shared vocabulary and analytic structure for a research community that has, to date, lacked the conceptual infrastructure to address trust calibration systematically.

The Bastani et al. (2025) finding --- that unrestricted AI access can harm learning through overtrust and cognitive offloading --- is not an anomaly. It is a predictable consequence of deploying capable AI systems in educational contexts without mechanisms for detecting and correcting trust miscalibration. The framework proposed here offers a principled, theoretically grounded approach to preventing such outcomes by treating trust calibration as a first-class design target rather than an afterthought.

The path forward is empirical. The conceptual architecture presented in this paper must be tested through design-based research (Anderson & Shattuck, 2012; McKenney & Reeves, 2019) that implements trust calibration scaffolds in authentic educational settings, measures their effects on calibration accuracy and learning outcomes, and iteratively refines the framework based on evidence. The research agenda outlined in Section 6.3 provides specific, testable propositions to guide this work.

As educational AI becomes increasingly autonomous and pervasive, the question for researchers and designers is no longer simply "Do learners trust AI?" but rather "Is their trust calibrated?" --- and critically, "Who or what calibrates it?" The answer to this question will determine whether the growing integration of AI in education produces learners who think critically with AI or learners who defer uncritically to it. Trust calibration is not an obstacle to AI adoption in education; it is the condition that makes AI adoption educationally defensible.
