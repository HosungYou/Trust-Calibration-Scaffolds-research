## 5. The Two-Level Trust Calibration Framework

This section presents the core contribution of this paper: a Two-Level Trust Calibration Framework that integrates learner-level trust dynamics (Level 1) with system-level oversight design (Level 2) through an adaptive calibration cycle. The framework is evidence-informed and conceptual; its novelty lies not in the individual components, each of which draws on established theory and evidence from related fields, but in their integration and application to the specific problem of trust calibration in educational AI environments.

### 5.1 From Fragmented Trust to Integrated Framework

As documented in Section 4, the current research landscape addresses trust dynamics and system design in isolation. Studies of learner trust describe how trust forms, changes, and influences behavior during AI interaction, but they do not prescribe how systems should respond when trust is miscalibrated. Conversely, studies of AI scaffolding and oversight design propose system architectures and pedagogical strategies but rarely incorporate the learner's trust state as a variable that shapes design decisions. No existing framework connects these two levels through a dynamic feedback mechanism that senses learner trust states and adjusts system behavior accordingly.

The Two-Level Trust Calibration Framework addresses this integration gap by drawing on four theoretical traditions. From trust-in-automation theory (Lee & See, 2004; Hoff & Bashir, 2015), it inherits the distinction between trust level and trust calibration, and the understanding that trust is a dynamic, multi-layered construct that must be evaluated against system reliability rather than treated as an unconditional good. From scaffolding theory (Wood et al., 1976), it inherits the principles of contingent support, fading, and transfer of responsibility --- principles that provide the pedagogical logic for adaptive oversight and that distinguish educationally grounded interventions from ad hoc design constraints. From self-regulated learning theory (Zimmerman, 2000; Winne & Hadwin, 1998), it inherits the cyclical model of performance, monitoring, evaluation, and adaptation that structures the calibration cycle and ensures that the framework captures the recursive, developmental nature of trust calibration. From adjacent field evidence on desirable difficulties (Bjork & Bjork, 2011), productive failure (Kapur, 2008, 2016), and trust repair in human-agent interaction (de Visser et al., 2020), it derives specific mechanisms that can be deployed as calibration scaffolds --- mechanisms with strong empirical grounding in their originating fields that await systematic application to the educational AI trust domain. The framework is thus integrative rather than additive: it does not simply combine existing theories but proposes a new architecture in which system-level design shapes the conditions under which learner-level calibration processes operate, and learner-level behavioral signals continuously inform system-level design adjustments.

### 5.2 Level 1 --- Learner Trust Dynamics (Micro)

Level 1 of the framework models the internal cognitive and metacognitive processes through which learners form, maintain, and revise trust judgments during interaction with AI systems. Its structural basis is adapted from Wang et al.'s (2025) integrated model of trust in AI-assisted learning, with a critical extension: the explicit incorporation of metacognitive processes as mediators of trust calibration, a dimension that Wang et al.'s Stimulus-Organism-Response architecture acknowledges but does not fully theorize.

**Trust antecedents.** AI system characteristics serve as the initial stimuli that shape trust formation. These include the transparency of AI reasoning processes, the observed accuracy of AI outputs, the design quality of the interface, and the consistency of AI behavior across interactions. These characteristics function as informational inputs from which learners construct mental models of AI reliability --- mental models that may be accurate or distorted depending on the learner's capacity for critical evaluation and the information the system makes available.

**Trust formation and metacognition.** At the core of Level 1 is the learner's trust state, conceptualized not as a static attitude but as a dynamic judgment that operates in conjunction with metacognitive processes. Drawing on Hoff and Bashir's (2015) three-layer model, trust at this level comprises three interacting layers: *dispositional trust*, reflecting stable individual differences in propensity to trust technology, shaped by personality, prior experience, and cultural context; *situational trust*, shaped by contextual factors including task difficulty, perceived stakes, time pressure, and immediate AI behavior; and *learned trust*, accumulated through direct experience with the AI system over time and subject to updating as interaction history accumulates. Critically, these trust layers do not operate in isolation from metacognition. The learner's capacity for self-monitoring, self-evaluation, and strategic regulation (Zimmerman, 2000) determines whether trust judgments are subjected to reflective scrutiny or accepted unreflectively --- a distinction that separates calibrated trust from the kind of uncritical reliance documented by Bastani et al. (2025).

**Internal calibration mechanisms.** Several learner-side processes contribute to the accuracy of trust judgments. Metacognitive monitoring enables learners to recognize discrepancies between their own understanding and AI-generated outputs, activating the evaluative processes that prevent automatic acceptance. Critical evaluation skills support the systematic assessment of AI output quality against domain knowledge and logical consistency. AI literacy, understood as knowledge of how AI systems function, their capabilities, and their characteristic failure modes (Long & Magerko, 2020), provides the domain-specific knowledge base necessary for informed trust judgments --- a learner who understands that large language models generate fluent text regardless of factual accuracy is better positioned to calibrate trust than one who equates fluency with reliability. Self-directed learning dispositions influence whether learners actively seek to verify and extend AI-provided information or passively consume it.

**Trust manifestation.** The behavioral output of the trust-metacognition interaction takes two primary forms: reliance (accepting, adopting, or building upon AI output) and resistance (rejecting, modifying, or disregarding AI output). Neither reliance nor resistance is inherently appropriate; their value depends on whether they are calibrated to the actual reliability of the AI system for the given task. Excessive reliance in the face of unreliable AI output constitutes overtrust --- the condition documented by Bastani et al. (2025) in which learner performance on unassisted tasks deteriorated following AI tutoring, as students bypassed the effortful cognitive processing that constitutes genuine learning. Excessive resistance in the face of reliable AI output constitutes undertrust, resulting in missed learning opportunities and inefficient allocation of cognitive resources (Parasuraman & Riley, 1997).

**Learning outcomes.** The downstream effects of calibrated or miscalibrated trust on knowledge construction, skill development, and transfer capacity constitute the ultimate dependent variable of Level 1. Overtrust degrades learning through cognitive offloading (Risko & Gilbert, 2016), reducing the effortful processing necessary for durable encoding and schema construction. Undertrust degrades learning through opportunity cost, denying learners access to potentially valuable scaffolding, feedback, and cognitive partnership.

A key limitation of Level 1 considered alone is that it is descriptive rather than prescriptive. It models what happens within the learner during AI interaction --- how trust forms, how it interacts with metacognition, and how it manifests in behavior --- but does not address how external systems should intervene when trust is miscalibrated. This limitation motivates Level 2.

### 5.3 Level 2 --- System-Mediated Trust Calibration (Macro)

Level 2 addresses the design question that Level 1 leaves unanswered: how should educational AI systems be structured to detect and correct miscalibrated trust? This level specifies six Trust Calibration Scaffolds, each grounded in established educational or psychological theory and each targeting a specific dimension of trust miscalibration. The term "scaffold" is used deliberately: these interventions are intended to be contingent (responsive to the learner's current trust state), fading (reduced as calibration improves), and responsibility-transferring (ultimately equipping learners to calibrate trust independently) --- the three defining properties of pedagogical scaffolding (Wood et al., 1976).

**Scaffold 1: Metacognitive Prompts.** Drawing on the metacognitive prompting literature (Schraw & Dennison, 1994; Bannert et al., 2009), this scaffold involves system-generated prompts that require learners to articulate, evaluate, or justify their own reasoning before, during, or after AI interaction. For example, a system might prompt learners to formulate their own hypothesis before viewing an AI-generated response, or to explain why they chose to accept or reject a particular AI suggestion. The mechanism targets overtrust by activating self-monitoring processes that might otherwise remain dormant during fluent AI interaction. Guo et al.'s (2022) meta-analysis of metacognitive scaffolding in technology-enhanced learning environments reported a moderate-to-strong aggregate effect (*g* = 0.50) on self-regulated learning outcomes and a meaningful effect (*g* = 0.40) on learning outcomes, providing robust evidence for the efficacy of this scaffold type in related contexts [CONFIRM WITH LITERATURE].

**Scaffold 2: Desirable Difficulties.** Grounded in Bjork and Bjork's (2011) desirable difficulties framework, this scaffold introduces deliberate challenges that impede immediate task performance but enhance long-term learning and trust calibration. In the context of AI-mediated learning, desirable difficulties may include requiring learners to produce initial solution attempts before AI assistance becomes available, introducing response delays that prevent reflexive AI reliance, or presenting AI outputs in formats that require active processing rather than passive consumption. The mechanism operates by restoring the effortful cognitive engagement that unconstrained AI access can eliminate. Importantly, the difficulties are "desirable" only insofar as they induce processing that strengthens durable learning; arbitrary or excessive difficulty serves no calibration function and risks producing frustration without pedagogical benefit.

**Scaffold 3: Productive Failure Points.** Based on Kapur's (2008, 2016) productive failure paradigm, this scaffold structures learning sequences so that learners first attempt problems without AI assistance, experience the boundaries of their own knowledge, and then receive AI support with explicit guidance on why and how the AI's contribution differs from or extends their own attempts. Productive failure has demonstrated effects up to twice those of conventional direct instruction in mathematics education (Kapur, 2016), and its mechanism --- generating and exploring incorrect or incomplete solutions before receiving instruction --- directly addresses both overtrust (by building awareness of one's own capabilities relative to AI) and undertrust (by demonstrating specific domains where AI assistance adds genuine value) [CONFIRM WITH LITERATURE].

**Scaffold 4: AI Uncertainty Transparency.** This scaffold involves the explicit communication of AI confidence levels, known limitations, and domain boundaries to learners. Drawing on the explainable AI (XAI) literature, AI uncertainty transparency enables learners to make informed trust judgments calibrated to the system's own assessment of its reliability, rather than relying on global impressions of AI competence that may not differentiate across task types or difficulty levels. Implementation may include confidence score visualizations, explicit domain limitation disclosures, or comparative accuracy information across task types. The goal is not to reduce trust indiscriminately but to provide the information necessary for appropriately differentiated trust --- higher trust in domains where the AI is demonstrably reliable and appropriately tempered trust in domains where it is not. This scaffold is grounded in Lee and See's (2004) foundational argument that appropriate trust requires information about system capabilities and limitations.

**Scaffold 5: Socratic Dialogue.** Rooted in one of the oldest and most enduring pedagogical traditions, Socratic dialogue as implemented in AI systems involves AI-initiated questioning that probes learner understanding rather than providing direct answers. When a learner requests AI assistance, the system responds with questions designed to activate the learner's own reasoning processes: "What do you think the answer might be?", "What evidence supports your current approach?", "Can you identify where your reasoning might be going wrong?" This scaffold shifts the fundamental interaction mode from answer-giving to thinking-prompting, disrupting the cognitive offloading dynamic that characterizes overtrust while maintaining the learner's engagement with AI as a productive intellectual partner. The effectiveness of questioning over telling in promoting deeper learning is well established in the tutoring literature (Chi et al., 2001), though its specific application to trust calibration in AI-mediated contexts remains to be empirically tested.

**Scaffold 6: Progressive Autonomy Release.** Adapted from Wood et al.'s (1976) fading principle and elaborated by Pea (2004), this scaffold implements the gradual reduction of scaffold intensity as evidence of improved trust calibration accumulates. Progressive autonomy release embodies the core property of pedagogical scaffolding: support is inherently temporary, provided at maximum intensity when the learner needs it and systematically withdrawn as the learner develops the capacity for self-regulated performance. In the trust calibration context, this means that learners who demonstrate calibrated trust --- appropriately differentiating their reliance on AI across reliability contexts --- progressively receive less external prompting, fewer imposed difficulties, and greater autonomy in their AI interactions. Without fading, scaffolding risks creating dependence on the scaffold itself rather than developing the learner's internal calibration capacity.

Two important qualifications apply to Level 2 as a whole. First, adjacent field evidence substantially strengthens the theoretical grounding of these scaffolds. De Visser et al.'s (2020) work on trust repair in human-robot interaction demonstrates that calibration interventions can effectively restore appropriate trust following automation failures, providing a conceptual bridge to the educational AI context. However, direct empirical evidence for these specific scaffolds deployed specifically for trust calibration in educational AI is sparse. The framework is evidence-informed in the sense that its components draw on well-established theory and evidence from related fields, but it is conceptual in the sense that the integrated application to educational AI trust calibration has not yet been empirically tested. This honest positioning as an evidence-informed conceptual framework, consistent with the legitimate purposes of critical reviews as articulated by Grant and Booth (2009) and Peters et al. (2020), is essential to the framework's scholarly integrity. Second, the six scaffolds are not intended as an exhaustive taxonomy; they represent the mechanisms for which the strongest theoretical and empirical grounding can currently be identified. Future research may identify additional scaffold types as the evidence base for trust calibration in educational AI develops.

### 5.4 The Adaptive Calibration Cycle

The Adaptive Calibration Cycle constitutes the central engine of the framework, connecting the descriptive model of Level 1 with the prescriptive design of Level 2 through a continuous, dynamic feedback process. Aligned with the cyclical structure of self-regulated learning theory (Zimmerman, 2000), the cycle operates through four stages that repeat iteratively throughout the learning interaction.

**Stage 1 --- PERFORMANCE.** The learner interacts with the AI system, generating observable behavioral data. These behaviors --- including AI output acceptance or rejection decisions, the extent and nature of modifications made to AI suggestions, the time invested in examining and verifying AI outputs, information-seeking actions triggered by AI recommendations, and patterns of AI use across different task types and difficulty levels --- constitute the empirical basis from which trust states can be inferred. The performance stage does not require the learner to explicitly report their trust; rather, trust is indexed through its behavioral manifestations, an approach consistent with the behavioral indicators of trust established in the automation literature (Lee & See, 2004; Dzindolet et al., 2003).

**Stage 2 --- MONITORING.** The system detects patterns in behavioral data that signal the learner's current trust state. This stage is functionally analogous to the self-monitoring phase in SRL theory, but with a critical distinction: the monitoring is performed *by the system* about *the learner*, rather than by the learner about themselves. Monitoring metrics include the ratio of AI output acceptance to rejection, the proportion of AI suggestions adopted without modification, the average time spent examining AI outputs before acting on them, the frequency and depth of verification behaviors, and the consistency of these patterns across task types and AI reliability levels. The system's monitoring function supplements, rather than replaces, the learner's own metacognitive monitoring; both forms of monitoring are necessary for effective calibration, and the framework is designed to progressively transfer monitoring responsibility from the system to the learner as calibration improves.

**Stage 3 --- EVALUATION.** Based on monitored behavioral patterns, the system (or, in less automated implementations, the educator) evaluates the learner's trust state along three categories: overtrust, undertrust, or calibrated. The evaluation is inherently relational, requiring comparison of the learner's trust-indexed behaviors against the AI system's actual reliability profile for the relevant task domain. A learner who consistently accepts AI outputs without verification is not necessarily overtrusting; if the AI is genuinely reliable for those tasks, the behavior may reflect calibrated efficiency rather than complacency. The same behavior directed at unreliable AI outputs, however, constitutes overtrust. This context-sensitivity is essential and distinguishes trust calibration assessment from simple behavioral frequency counting. The Trust-Reliability Matrix introduced below provides the evaluative framework for this stage.

**Stage 4 --- ADAPTATION.** Based on the evaluation, scaffold intensity is adjusted through one of three responses. If overtrust is detected, scaffolds are intensified: desirable difficulties are introduced or increased, metacognitive prompts are activated, Socratic dialogue replaces direct answers, and productive failure opportunities are structured into the learning sequence. If undertrust is detected, transparency scaffolds are deployed: AI confidence information is made visible, guided comparison tasks demonstrate AI reliability in specific domains, and productive failure experiences are designed to highlight domains where AI assistance adds genuine value. If calibrated trust is detected, progressive autonomy release begins: scaffold intensity is gradually reduced, the learner is granted greater autonomy in AI interactions, and the system transitions toward a monitoring-only mode that can re-engage scaffolds if calibration deteriorates.

The four stages are formalized in a Dynamic Scaffolding Principle: as calibration improves, oversight decreases through scaffold fading and expanded learner autonomy; as calibration deteriorates, oversight increases through scaffold intensification and reintroduction of structured support. This bidirectional responsiveness distinguishes the framework from static oversight designs that apply uniform constraints regardless of learner state --- a one-size-fits-all approach that is either too intrusive for well-calibrated learners or insufficiently protective for miscalibrated ones.

**Figure 2** presents the Two-Level Framework visually, depicting Level 2 (System-Mediated Trust Calibration) in the upper register, Level 1 (Learner Trust Dynamics) in the lower register, and the Adaptive Calibration Cycle as a central band connecting the two. Bidirectional arrows indicate the sensing pathway (behavioral data flowing upward from Level 1 to inform the cycle's monitoring and evaluation stages) and the adjusting pathway (scaffold modifications flowing downward from the cycle to alter the learner's interaction conditions). This visual architecture makes explicit the framework's central claim: that effective trust calibration requires continuous, bidirectional communication between the learner's internal trust dynamics and the system's external oversight design.

**Figure 3** introduces the Trust-Reliability Calibration Matrix, a 2 x 2 analytic tool mapping learner trust (high vs. low) against AI reliability (high vs. low) to produce four distinct calibration states, each with different implications for learning and different scaffold responses.

*Quadrant I: High Trust + High AI Reliability = Calibrated Appropriate Use.* Trust is warranted because the AI is genuinely reliable in the given domain. This represents the target state and the condition under which progressive autonomy release (fading) is appropriate. Continued heavy scaffolding in this quadrant would be paternalistic and counterproductive to the development of self-regulated AI use.

*Quadrant II: High Trust + Low AI Reliability = Overtrust ("Bastani Trap").* This is the most dangerous miscalibration state. The learner trusts AI outputs that are unreliable for the purpose that matters most --- durable learning --- resulting in cognitive offloading, reduced effortful processing, and consequent skill atrophy. The learning deterioration observed by Bastani et al. (2025) represents precisely this quadrant: high learner trust paired with functionally unreliable AI outputs for novel problem types requiring independent reasoning, with no calibration mechanism to correct the misalignment. The prescribed scaffold response is intensification --- deployment of desirable difficulties, productive failure tasks, and metacognitive prompts to restore the effortful processing that overtrust eliminates.

*Quadrant III: Low Trust + High AI Reliability = Undertrust / Missed Benefit.* The learner's skepticism, while epistemically virtuous in Quadrant II, is maladaptive here. The AI system is genuinely reliable, but the learner does not benefit from it because trust is insufficient to support productive engagement. Prescribed scaffold responses include AI uncertainty transparency (making reliability evidence visible), guided comparison tasks that allow learners to verify AI accuracy directly, and productive failure experiences designed to demonstrate domains where AI assistance genuinely outperforms unaided performance.

*Quadrant IV: Low Trust + Low AI Reliability = Calibrated Avoidance.* The learner's low trust accurately reflects low AI reliability, representing appropriate skepticism rather than a failure of calibration. The scaffold response is maintenance: reinforce the verification habits and metacognitive processes that produce accurate calibration, build AI literacy to support ongoing discrimination between reliable and unreliable AI contexts, and monitor for potential drift toward overtrust as AI systems improve or as the novelty of appropriate skepticism fades.

**The Initialization Principle.** A potential concern with the Adaptive Calibration Cycle is circularity: the system must know the learner's calibration state to select appropriate scaffolds, but the calibration state is itself shaped by scaffolding. This concern is addressed through an explicit initialization principle. When the learner's calibration state is unknown --- as at the beginning of any new learning interaction --- the system defaults to high oversight, deploying scaffolds at maximum intensity. As behavioral data accumulates through the performance and monitoring stages, the system progressively refines its model of the learner's trust state and adjusts scaffold intensity accordingly. This initialization logic mirrors the foundational scaffolding principle of providing maximum support initially and fading as learner competence develops (Wood et al., 1976). The apparent circularity is thus reframed as a design feature: the system operates adaptively under uncertainty, converging on appropriate calibration through iterative observation and adjustment rather than requiring a priori knowledge of learner states.

Several observations emerge from this evidence mapping. First, the individual scaffold components are grounded in established, well-replicated research traditions. Metacognitive prompting, desirable difficulties, productive failure, and scaffolding fading each have substantial empirical support within their originating fields. Second, the evidence base weakens at two specific points: the application of these mechanisms to AI-specific trust calibration (as opposed to general learning enhancement), and the integration of learner-level dynamics with system-level design through the adaptive cycle. These are precisely the points at which the framework makes its novel contribution, and they are also the points at which empirical validation is most urgently needed. Third, the honest identification of these evidence gaps is itself a contribution, delineating the precise empirical questions that future research must address to move from evidence-informed conceptual framework to evidence-based prescriptive model.

The framework's novelty lies in the integration and application to educational AI trust, not in the individual components. Each scaffold draws on established theory and evidence from related fields; the contribution is the systematic synthesis that connects learner trust dynamics, calibration scaffolds, and adaptive oversight design within a unified conceptual architecture. This positioning as an evidence-informed conceptual framework --- rather than a fully evidence-based prescriptive model --- reflects both intellectual honesty and strategic alignment with the critical review methodology, which legitimately supports the identification of conceptual gaps and the proposal of integrative frameworks to address them (Grant & Booth, 2009; Peters et al., 2020).
