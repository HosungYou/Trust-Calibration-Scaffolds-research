## CATEGORY 1: ChatGPT Overtrust / Overreliance in Education

### Case 1.1 — Bastani et al. (2024/2025): Generative AI Harms Learning in Math (Wharton/UPenn)

**Full citation:**
> Bastani, H., Bastani, O., Sungu, A., Ge, H., Kabakci, O., & Mariman, R. (2025). Generative AI without guardrails can harm learning: Evidence from high school mathematics. *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences*. https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.2414972122

*(Note: Initially posted as SSRN working paper July 2024: https://ssrn.com/abstract=4895486; subsequently published in PNAS 2025; a correction was also issued: https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.2518204122)*

**Key finding:** In a randomized experiment with ~1,000 Turkish high school students (grades 9–11), students with unrestricted ChatGPT access solved 48% more practice problems correctly but scored 17% **worse** on the post-test compared to control students. A guardrailed "hint-only" tutor version improved practice performance by 127% but showed no post-test benefit. Students who used ChatGPT as a crutch failed to internalize the material.

**How to use in manuscript:**
- **Hook / opening evidence**: Clearest empirical demonstration that unrestricted AI access creates a performance paradox — students appear to succeed while actually failing to learn.
- **Evidence for calibration gap**: Students over-trusted the AI's answers, did not engage in productive struggle, and developed false confidence. The guardrailed condition demonstrates that scaffold design matters critically.
- **Section fit**: Introduction, Section on "Consequences of Overtrust," Discussion.

**Source URLs:**
- PNAS: https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.2414972122
- SSRN preprint: https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=4895486
- Wharton summary: https://knowledge.wharton.upenn.edu/article/without-guardrails-generative-ai-can-harm-education/
- Hechinger Report coverage: https://hechingerreport.org/kids-chatgpt-worse-on-tests/

### Case 1.2 — Anthropic (2025): University Students Offload Higher-Order Cognition to Claude

**Full citation:**
> Anthropic. (2025). *How university students use Claude: An Anthropic education report*. Anthropic. https://www.anthropic.com/news/anthropic-education-report-how-university-students-use-claude

**Key finding:** Analysis of university student interactions with Claude found that 39.8% of uses involved the AI performing "Creating" tasks and 30.2% "Analyzing" tasks (Bloom's Taxonomy), meaning students were disproportionately offloading the highest-order cognitive functions. Students asked for direct answers almost half the time with minimal back-and-forth. Anthropic's own researchers flagged this as raising "questions about ensuring students don't offload critical cognitive tasks to AI systems."

**How to use in manuscript:**
- **Evidence for calibration gap**: Even the AI provider's data shows the problem — students are not using AI as a scaffold but as a replacement for cognition.
- **Industry acknowledgment**: Strengthens the argument that this is not alarmism; it is recognized by practitioners.
- **Section fit**: Motivation for intervention, Discussion.

**Source URL:** https://www.anthropic.com/news/anthropic-education-report-how-university-students-use-claude
**Coverage:** https://hechingerreport.org/proof-points-offload-critical-thinking-ai/

### Case 1.3 — Gerlich (2025): AI Tool Use Correlates with Reduced Critical Thinking Scores

**Full citation:**
> Gerlich, M. (2025). AI tools in society: Impacts on cognitive offloading and the future of critical thinking. *Societies*, *15*(1), 6. https://doi.org/10.3390/soc15010006

**Key finding:** A study found a significant negative correlation between frequent AI tool usage and critical thinking abilities, mediated by increased cognitive offloading. Younger participants exhibited higher AI dependence and lower critical thinking scores compared to older participants. The mechanism is habitual cognitive offloading that degrades metacognitive monitoring.

**How to use in manuscript:**
- **Mechanism evidence**: Explains *why* trust calibration matters — uncalibrated (over)trust leads to cognitive offloading, which degrades skills needed to evaluate AI output in the future.
- **Section fit**: Theoretical framework, literature review.

**Source URL:** https://www.mdpi.com/2075-4698/15/1/6

### Case 1.4 — Yeung et al. (2025/2026): "In ChatGPT They Trust" — Student Misuse and Automation Bias

**Full citation:**
> Yeung, L. K. C., Chow, D. P. L., Wong, P. H., & Lau, S. S. S. (2025). In ChatGPT they trust: A study of students' perceptions and misuse of ChatGPT in higher education. *AI and Ethics*, *6*, 11. https://doi.org/10.1007/s43681-025-00855-w

**Key finding:** A qualitative study of 20 Hong Kong university students found that misusers of ChatGPT showed more pronounced automation bias — uncritically accepting AI output — and a paradoxical pattern: even students aware of hallucinations continued to rely on ChatGPT, rationalizing that the burden of cross-checking was theirs to carry rather than re-evaluating their trust level.

**How to use in manuscript:**
- **Direct evidence of overtrust and calibration failure**: Documents the paradox of students knowing AI can be wrong yet persisting in over-reliance.
- **Automation bias terminology**: Supports framing the problem as "automation bias" in an academic context.
- **Section fit**: Literature review on overtrust mechanisms.

**Source URL:** https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s43681-025-00855-w

## CATEGORY 2: Medical / Law Education AI Cases

### Case 2.1 — Nguyen (2024): ChatGPT in Medical Education — A Precursor for Automation Bias?

**Full citation:**
> Nguyen, T. (2024). ChatGPT in medical education: A precursor for automation bias? *JMIR Medical Education*, *10*, e50174. https://doi.org/10.2196/50174

*(Also available via PMC: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10831594/)*

**Key finding:** Argues that because medical students are digitally proficient but clinically inexperienced, they are at heightened risk of automation bias — accepting AI diagnoses and treatment recommendations without adequate critical appraisal. The editorial warns that ChatGPT use in medical education must be limited to specific tasks and accompanied by AI literacy curriculum, or automation bias will compound over time into erroneous clinical outcomes.

**How to use in manuscript:**
- **High-stakes domain evidence**: Demonstrates that calibration failure is not merely an academic integrity problem — in medicine it risks patient harm.
- **Mechanism**: Articulates why *expertise asymmetry* (AI appears authoritative; student lacks the knowledge to critique it) drives overtrust.
- **Section fit**: Motivating the stakes of trust calibration; domain generalization argument.

**Source URL:** https://mededu.jmir.org/2024/1/e50174

### Case 2.3 — Mata v. Avianca, Inc. (2023): Lawyers Submit ChatGPT-Hallucinated Case Citations

**Full citation (legal case):**
> *Mata v. Avianca, Inc.*, 678 F.Supp.3d 443 (S.D.N.Y. 2023). (Case No. 1:22-cv-01461, decided June 22, 2023)

**Full citation (sanctions order):**
> Levidow, Levidow & Oberman P.C.; LoDuca, P.; Schwartz, S. A. — Sanctioned under Fed. R. Civ. P. 11. *See* Seyfarth Shaw LLP update: https://www.seyfarth.com/news-insights/update-on-the-chatgpt-case-counsel-who-submitted-fake-cases-are-sanctioned.html

**Key finding:** Attorneys Peter LoDuca and Steven Schwartz submitted a legal brief containing six fictitious court cases invented by ChatGPT — complete with fabricated names, docket numbers, and quotations. When the court challenged the citations, the lawyers asked ChatGPT to confirm the cases existed; the AI affirmed they did. Judge P. Kevin Castel imposed a $5,000 sanction, ruling the lawyers acted with "subjective bad faith." The case became the foundational example of professional overtrust in AI-generated legal content. By 2025, trackers identified more than 600 similar cases in U.S. courts.

*(For law education context: Stanford HAI's RegLab found that even specialized legal AI tools hallucinate 17–34% of the time: https://hai.stanford.edu/news/ai-trial-legal-models-hallucinate-1-out-6-or-more-benchmarking-queries)*

**How to use in manuscript:**
- **Most cited real-world overtrust case**: Universal recognition makes it a powerful hook.
- **Professional education implications**: Now taught in law schools as a cautionary case; directly informs how legal education must address AI trust calibration.
- **Evidence that hallucination + overtrust = serious consequences** even for trained professionals.
- **Section fit**: Opening hook, high-stakes examples, motivation.

**Source URLs:**
- Wikipedia summary: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mata_v._Avianca,_Inc.
- CNN: https://www.cnn.com/2023/05/27/business/chat-gpt-avianca-mata-lawyers
- Legal Dive: https://www.legaldive.com/news/chatgpt-fake-legal-cases-generative-ai-hallucinations/651557/
- Stanford HAI hallucination rate study: https://hai.stanford.edu/news/ai-trial-legal-models-hallucinate-1-out-6-or-more-benchmarking-queries

## CATEGORY 3: Teacher Challenges with AI Verification

### Case 3.1 — Fleckenstein et al. (2024): Teachers Cannot Reliably Detect AI-Generated Student Essays

**Full citation:**
> Fleckenstein, J., Meyer, J., Jansen, T., Keller, S., Koller, O., & Moller, J. (2024). Do teachers spot AI? Evaluating the detectability of AI-generated texts among student essays. *Computers and Education: Artificial Intelligence*, *6*, 100209. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.caeai.2024.100209

**Key finding:** In a study with 89 pre-service teachers and 200 experienced teachers, neither group could reliably identify AI-generated text in student essays. Pre-service teachers performed essentially at chance regardless of text quality; experienced teachers performed better only with high-quality texts. Human detection rates for AI text are only slightly above chance (true positive rate ~24%). This creates a verification asymmetry: students know what AI produced their work, but teachers cannot tell.

**How to use in manuscript:**
- **Teacher verification failure as a trust calibration problem**: Teachers cannot calibrate their suspicion of AI use because they lack the verification tools to do so. This mirrors the student-side calibration problem.
- **Direct citation in Computers and Education: AI** — highly relevant for the journal audience.
- **Section fit**: "Trust from the educator perspective," Assessment challenges section.

**Source URL:** https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X24000109

### Case 3.2 — Weber-Wulff et al. (2023): AI Detection Tools Are Neither Accurate Nor Reliable

**Full citation:**
> Weber-Wulff, D., Anohina-Naumeca, A., Bjelobaba, S., Foltynek, T., Guerrero-Dib, J., Popoola, O., Sigut, P., & Waddington, L. (2023). Testing of detection tools for AI-generated text. *International Journal for Educational Integrity*, *19*, 26. https://doi.org/10.1007/s40979-023-00146-z

**Key finding:** Systematic testing of commercially available AI detection tools found they are neither accurate nor reliable. The main error direction is classifying AI-generated text as human-written (false negatives), meaning tools provide teachers with false reassurance. Detection tools also systematically disadvantage non-native English speakers.

**How to use in manuscript:**
- **Structural verification gap**: Even if teachers want to verify AI use, the tools provided to them are unreliable — a system-level trust calibration failure.
- **Policy relevance**: Argues for pedagogical redesign rather than technological detection.
- **Section fit**: Teacher challenges, Assessment integrity, Implications section.

**Source URL:** https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40979-023-00146-z

### Case 3.3 — NPR (2025): Teachers Using AI Detection Software — What Happens When It's Wrong?

**Full citation:**
> Nadworny, E. (2025, December 16). Teachers are using software to see if students used AI. What happens when it's wrong? *NPR*. https://www.npr.org/2025/12/16/nx-s1-5492397/ai-schools-teachers-students

**Key finding:** NPR documents specific cases of students wrongly accused of AI writing by detection software, including a student told that phrases like "in addition to" and "in contrast" were flagged as AI "signpost phrases." False accusations particularly affect students with distinctive writing styles and non-native English speakers. The Guardian's FOI investigation (UK) found nearly 7,000 confirmed AI-related cheating cases in 2023–24, up from 1.6 per 1,000 to 5.1 per 1,000 students in one year.

**How to use in manuscript:**
- **Human interest / concrete illustration**: Specific stories of false accusation make the verification problem tangible.
- **Bidirectional calibration failure**: Not only are students miscalibrated about AI; teachers/institutions are miscalibrated about detection tools.
- **Section fit**: Introduction or teacher challenges section.

## CATEGORY 4: Policy Reports

### Policy 4.1 — EU AI Act (2024): Education as High-Risk Domain

**Full citation:**
> European Parliament and Council of the European Union. (2024). *Regulation (EU) 2024/1689 of the European Parliament and of the Council of 13 June 2024 laying down harmonised rules on artificial intelligence (Artificial Intelligence Act)*. Official Journal of the European Union. https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=CELEX:32024R1689

**Specific provisions for education:**
- **Recital 56**: AI systems in education or vocational training for determining access/admission, assigning persons to educational programs, evaluating learning outcomes, assessing appropriate education levels, and monitoring prohibited behavior during tests are classified as **high-risk AI systems** because they "may determine the educational and professional course of a person's life."
- **Annex III, Section 3**: Explicitly lists educational AI systems as high-risk.
- **Prohibited practice**: Emotion recognition systems in educational contexts are prohibited.
- **Implementation timeline**: High-risk rules come into force August 2026–2027.

**How to use in manuscript:**
- **Policy context**: Positions the manuscript within the current regulatory landscape. The EU has formally recognized that AI in education carries high risk — trust calibration scaffolds are a direct response to what regulators have flagged.
- **Justification for urgency**: The regulatory clock is running; institutions need trust calibration frameworks now.
- **Section fit**: Policy context, Introduction, Discussion/Implications.

**Source URLs:**
- Official full text: https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=CELEX:32024R1689
- Recital 56: https://artificialintelligenceact.eu/recital/56/
- Annex III: https://artificialintelligenceact.eu/annex/3/
- University implications: https://www.digitaleducationcouncil.com/post/eu-ai-act-what-it-means-for-universities

> Perkins, M., Roe, J., Postma, D., McGaughran, J., & Hickerson, D. (2024). Cheating in the age of generative AI: A high school survey study of cheating behaviors before and after the release of ChatGPT. *Computers and Education: Artificial Intelligence*, *6*, 100210. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.caeai.2024.100210

**Key finding:** Survey data from high school students showed that while overall self-reported cheating rates did not dramatically increase post-ChatGPT, qualitative responses indicated a normalization of AI use for assessment tasks without critical evaluation. Students were not calibrating their use of AI to task requirements — many used AI even for tasks it consistently fails.

**How to use in manuscript:**
- **High school context data**: Extends the calibration problem to secondary education.
- **Directly in Computers & Education: AI**: Core journal target.
- **Section fit**: Literature review, scope of problem.

### Paper 5.4 — Trust and Reliance on AI: Costs of Overreliance (Computers in Human Behavior, 2024)

**Full citation:**
> Vossing, M., Kuchler, M., & Satzger, G. (2024). Trust and reliance on AI: An experimental study on the extent and costs of overreliance on AI. *Computers in Human Behavior*, *157*, 108352. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.chb.2024.108352

**Key finding:** Experimental study showing that "the mere knowledge of advice being generated by an AI causes people to overrely on it" — participants followed AI advice even when it contradicted available contextual information and their own judgment. Overreliance was more pronounced than underreliance. Provides a controlled experimental basis for the overtrust claim.

**How to use in manuscript:**
- **Experimental evidence for automation bias**: Clean causal design establishes that AI labeling itself drives overtrust beyond any actual AI quality signal.
- **Section fit**: Theoretical framework, mechanism of trust miscalibration.

**Source URL:** https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0747563224002206

### Paper 5.5 — Effects of Over-Reliance on AI Dialogue Systems on Cognitive Abilities (Systematic Review, 2024)

**Full citation:**
> Luo, H., Han, Z., & He, F. (2024). The effects of over-reliance on AI dialogue systems on students' cognitive abilities: A systematic review. *Smart Learning Environments*, *11*, 59. https://doi.org/10.1186/s40561-024-00316-7

**Key finding:** Systematic review of 41 studies finding that over-reliance on AI dialogue systems correlates with measurable declines in critical thinking, writing quality, and independent problem-solving among students. The review identifies lack of trust calibration — students cannot accurately assess when AI is reliable — as a root mechanism. Calls for scaffolding that teaches students to evaluate AI outputs rather than accept them.

**How to use in manuscript:**
- **Systematic review authority**: Synthesizes the evidence base for cognitive impacts of AI over-reliance.
- **Explicit calibration framing**: Uses trust calibration language, making it directly citable.
- **Section fit**: Literature review, theoretical framework, outcome measures.

**Source URL:** https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s40561-024-00316-7
