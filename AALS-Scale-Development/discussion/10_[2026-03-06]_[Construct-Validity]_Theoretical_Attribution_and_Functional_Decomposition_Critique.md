# Discussion 10: Construct Validity Critique — Theoretical Attribution, Functional Decomposition, and Reification Risk

**Date:** 2026-03-06
**Status:** Critical revision required — affects manuscript framing, Figure 1, and interpretive claims
**Trigger:** Author self-critique during manuscript review
**Impact Level:** HIGH — affects theoretical framework presentation, figure design, discussion section

---

## 1. Problem Identification

### 1.1 The Core Critique

The current TCRS manuscript extracts a **single subfactor** from each of three broad theoretical traditions and uses it to represent the entire theory within the process model:

| Source Theory | Full Scope | What TCRS Extracts | Current Label in Figure 1 |
|---------------|-----------|---------------------|--------------------------|
| Metacognition (Winne & Hadwin, 1998) | 4 phases of SRL, multiple monitoring & control processes, metacognitive knowledge, metacognitive experiences | Trust state monitoring only | "METACOGNITION" |
| Trust Dynamics (Lee & See, 2004) | Trust formation, erosion, repair, transfer, dispositional vs. situational trust, multiple antecedents | Trust-reliability matching evaluation only | "TRUST EVALUATION" |
| Human Agency (Bandura, 2001) | Intentionality, forethought, self-reactiveness, self-reflectiveness, collective agency, proxy agency | Behavioral verification & adjustment only | "HUMAN AGENCY" |

### 1.2 Three Specific Threats Identified

**Threat 1: Construct Underrepresentation (Messick, 1995)**
Each subscale measures a narrow mechanism but is presented as grounded in the full scope of its source theory. This creates a gap between theoretical claims and measurement reality.

**Threat 2: Theoretical Attribution Overstatement**
Figure 1b labels each process component with the full theory name and citation (e.g., "METACOGNITION — Winne & Hadwin, 1998"), implying theoretical integration of the complete framework rather than selective borrowing of one mechanism.

**Threat 3: Reification Risk (Borsboom, 2005)**
If the TCRS is used diagnostically (e.g., "this learner has low Calibration Awareness"), practitioners may interpret through the lens of the full source theory (e.g., "this learner has deficient metacognition"), leading to over-generalized interventions.

### 1.3 Initially Cited Parallels — Corrected

Initial examples cited as precedent (PsyCap, MSLQ, Grit) were found to be **imprecise parallels** upon closer examination:

| Scale | What It Actually Does | Parallel to TCRS? |
|-------|----------------------|-------------------|
| PsyCap (Luthans et al., 2007) | Combines four **complete, independently validated constructs** into a higher-order construct | **No** — construct-level combination, not mechanism-level extraction |
| MSLQ (Pintrich et al., 1993) | Organizes **individually identifiable learning strategies** under a theoretical umbrella | **Partial** — each subscale is a complete construct, not a subfactor extraction |
| Grit (Duckworth et al., 2007) | Extracts perseverance from conscientiousness and relabels it | **Cautionary parallel** — received exactly the criticism TCRS should avoid (Credé et al., 2017) |

## 2. Corrected Precedent Analysis

### 2.1 Accurate Parallels: Functional Decomposition Models

The precise pattern TCRS uses — **extracting specific mechanisms from different theories and recombining them to serve functional roles in a new target construct** — has well-established precedent in the following models:

#### Technology Acceptance Model (TAM) — Davis (1989)

| Source Theory | Extracted Mechanism | Functional Role in TAM |
|---------------|--------------------|-----------------------|
| Expectancy Theory (Vroom, 1964) | Outcome expectancy | → "Perceived Usefulness" |
| Self-Efficacy Theory (Bandura, 1977) | Computer self-efficacy | → "Perceived Ease of Use" |

- Two mechanisms from two different theories, recombined into a new predictive model
- 50,000+ citations; one of the most validated models in Information Systems
- **Key:** TAM does NOT claim to "integrate expectancy theory and self-efficacy theory." It defines new constructs (PU, PEOU) and notes their theoretical origins as explanatory resources.

#### Theory of Planned Behavior (TPB) — Ajzen (1991)

| Source Theory | Extracted Mechanism | Functional Role in TPB |
|---------------|--------------------|-----------------------|
| Attitude theory (Fishbein & Ajzen, 1975) | Attitude toward behavior | → Attitudinal predictor of intention |
| Social influence theory | Subjective norms | → Social predictor of intention |
| Self-efficacy theory (Bandura, 1977) | Perceived behavioral control | → Control predictor of intention |

- Three mechanisms from three traditions, combined into a behavioral intention model
- 100,000+ citations; among the most cited models in all of social science
- **Key:** TPB presents itself as a model of behavioral intention, not as a synthesis of three source theories.

#### Transactional Model of Stress — Lazarus & Folkman (1984)

| Source Theory | Extracted Mechanism | Functional Role |
|---------------|--------------------|-----------------------|
| Cognitive appraisal theory | Primary appraisal (threat/challenge) | → "Is this threatening?" |
| Coping resource theory | Secondary appraisal (resource assessment) | → "Can I cope?" |
| Behavioral regulation theory | Coping strategies | → Actual coping behavior |

- **Structurally isomorphic to TCRS**: Appraisal → Evaluation → Action
- Sequential process model with feedback loops
- Among the most influential models in health psychology

#### TPACK — Mishra & Koehler (2006)

| Source Domain | Extracted Knowledge Type | Functional Role |
|---------------|------------------------|-----------------|
| Subject matter expertise | Content Knowledge (CK) | → What to teach |
| Pedagogical theory | Pedagogical Knowledge (PK) | → How to teach |
| Educational technology | Technological Knowledge (TK) | → Tools for teaching |

- Three knowledge types from different domains, combined into intersection constructs
- Most widely used framework in educational technology research

### 2.2 Success Conditions Across All Precedents

| Condition | TAM | TPB | Lazarus & Folkman | TPACK | TCRS (current) | TCRS (needed) |
|-----------|-----|-----|-------------------|-------|----------------|---------------|
| 1. Logical necessity from target construct | Yes | Yes | Yes | Yes | Yes | Yes |
| 2. Source theories as resources, not pillars | Yes | Yes | Yes | Yes | **No** | Must fix |
| 3. New construct names (not source theory names) | Yes | Yes | Yes | Yes | Partial | Must strengthen |
| 4. Empirical testability | Yes | Yes | Yes | Yes | Planned | Study 1/2 |
| 5. Boundary statements about extraction scope | Yes | Yes | Yes | Yes | **Weak** | Must add |

## 3. Diagnosis of Current Manuscript Deficiencies

### 3.1 Figure 1b — Theoretical Attribution Overstatement

**Current Figure 1b labels:**
```
METACOGNITION (Winne & Hadwin, 1998)
        ↓
TRUST EVALUATION (Lee & See, 2004)
        ↓
HUMAN AGENCY (Bandura, 2001)
        ↓
CALIBRATION (Stone, 2000)
```

**Problem:** The box labels use the FULL THEORY NAME as the primary label, with citations that point to the entire theoretical framework. This visually and rhetorically claims integration of complete theories.

### 3.2 Body Text — Inconsistent Framing

The manuscript oscillates between two framings:

| Framing | Example | Frequency |
|---------|---------|-----------|
| **Theory-as-pillar** (problematic) | "Drawing on Winne and Hadwin's (1998) model of self-regulated learning, this stage encompasses..." | Dominant |
| **Theory-as-resource** (appropriate) | "We do not claim to import the full scope of any single framework; rather, each theory contributes a specific explanatory function" | One paragraph only |

The correct framing appears exactly once (Section 2.1, paragraph 3) but is contradicted by the dominant rhetorical pattern throughout the manuscript.

### 3.3 Practical Implications — Diagnostic Over-interpretation

Current text: "students with low awareness may benefit from reflective exercises about their AI use patterns"

This implies that a low CA-Aw score diagnoses a specific type of metacognitive deficit and prescribes a specific type of intervention. This exceeds what the scale can claim.

## 4. Revision Plan

### Phase 1: Reframe Theoretical Attribution (Manuscript Text)

**Principle:** Source theories are explanatory resources for the target construct's functional components, not structural pillars of the framework.

#### 4.1 Section 2.1 — "Calibration as a Process, Not a Trait"

**ADD** a "Selective Theoretical Borrowing" table:

| Target Construct Function | Borrowed Mechanism | Source | What Is NOT Borrowed |
|--------------------------|-------------------|--------|---------------------|
| Monitoring capacity | Self-monitoring of trust state | Metacognitive monitoring (Winne & Hadwin, 1998, Phases 1-3) | Knowledge of cognition, metacognitive regulation strategies, SRL Phase 4 adaptation |
| Evaluative capacity | Trust-reliability matching assessment | Trust dynamics (Lee & See, 2004) | Trust formation, trust repair, dispositional trust, organizational trust antecedents |
| Regulatory capacity | Intentional verification & adjustment behaviors | Behavioral self-regulation (Bandura, 2001, self-reactiveness) | Intentionality, forethought, collective agency, proxy agency, moral agency |

**REVISE** the existing "selective borrowing" paragraph from a single disclaimer into a structural organizing principle that governs all subsequent descriptions.

#### 4.2 Sections 2.2 and 2.4 — Process Model and Subscale Descriptions

**CHANGE** the rhetorical pattern from:
> "**Metacognition (Foundation).** The process begins with self-monitoring: the learner's awareness... Drawing on Winne and Hadwin's (1998) model of self-regulated learning, this stage encompasses..."

**TO:**
> "**Monitoring Capacity (Foundation).** The calibration process begins with the learner's awareness of their own trust state... This monitoring function is informed by the concept of self-monitoring within self-regulated learning theory (Winne & Hadwin, 1998, Phases 1-3), specifically the learner's capacity to observe and represent their own cognitive states. Note that we borrow the monitoring mechanism specifically; the broader SRL framework's planning, enacting, and adapting processes are beyond the scope of this model."

**Apply this pattern consistently to all three components.**

#### 4.3 Section 2.4 — Subscale Names

**RETAIN** current names (Calibration Awareness, Calibration Judgment, Calibration Action) — the "Calibration" prefix provides adequate domain specificity.

**ADD** explicit statements after each subscale definition:
> "CA-Aw measures trust state monitoring in the context of AI interaction; it does not claim to measure metacognitive awareness in general, nor does a low CA-Aw score indicate a general metacognitive deficit."

### Phase 3: Add Interpretive Safeguards

#### 4.4 Discussion — Limitations

**ADD** a dedicated paragraph on interpretive boundaries:

> "Importantly, the TCRS subscale scores should not be interpreted as indicators of general metacognitive ability (CA-Aw), general critical thinking (CA-Jd), or general behavioral agency (CA-Ac). Each subscale measures a domain-specific capacity within the trust calibration context. A learner who scores low on Calibration Awareness may have excellent metacognitive skills in other domains but lack awareness of their trust dynamics with AI specifically. Accordingly, the TCRS is best understood as a screening instrument for trust calibration readiness, not a diagnostic tool for broader psychological constructs."

#### 4.5 Discussion — Practical Implications

**REVISE** intervention recommendations to be calibration-specific rather than theory-general:

**Current:** "students with low awareness may benefit from reflective exercises about their AI use patterns"

**Revised:** "students who score low on Calibration Awareness may benefit from structured prompts that direct attention to their trust state during AI interaction (e.g., 'How much are you trusting this AI output right now?'), rather than general metacognitive training"

### Phase 4: Strengthen Theoretical Justification

#### 4.6 Add a "Why These Three Functions?" Argument

**ADD** to Section 2.2 (before or after the process model description):

> "The three functional capacities are not selected because they happen to map onto available theories; rather, they are derived from the logical requirements of the target construct. Trust calibration, by definition, requires: (a) awareness of one's current trust state (without knowing one's position, adjustment is impossible), (b) evaluation of whether that state is appropriate (without a criterion, direction of adjustment is unknown), and (c) intentional behavioral adjustment (without action, awareness and evaluation remain inert). This necessity argument parallels the functional decomposition in other multi-source models: the Theory of Planned Behavior requires attitudinal, normative, and control components because behavioral intention logically requires all three (Ajzen, 1991); the Transactional Model of Stress requires appraisal and coping components because stress response logically requires both (Lazarus & Folkman, 1984)."
