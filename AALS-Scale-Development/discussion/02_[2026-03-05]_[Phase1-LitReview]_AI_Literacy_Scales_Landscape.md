# Phase 1: Literature Review — AI Literacy Scales Landscape

**Date:** 2026-03-05
**Purpose:** Systematic mapping of existing AI literacy scales to identify gaps and position AALS
**Method:** Academic web search (March 2026)

---

## 1. Systematic Review of AI Literacy Scales

### 1.1 Lintner (2024) — Comprehensive Review

**Source:** Lintner, C. (2024). A systematic review of AI literacy scales. *npj Science of Learning, 9*, 55.
- **Scope:** 22 studies validating 16 distinct scales
- **Populations:** General public, higher education students, secondary students, teachers
- **Key Finding:** Most scales demonstrate good structural validity and internal consistency, but few have been tested for content validity, reliability, construct validity, and responsiveness
- **Critical Gap:** No scales have been tested for cross-cultural validity
- **Scale Types:** Primarily self-assessment; some recent performance-based tests

### 1.2 Implications for AALS
The review confirms that AI literacy measurement is a crowded but shallow field — many scales exist, but most lack rigorous psychometric validation beyond EFA/CFA. No existing scale addresses trust calibration or the automation-agent distinction.

## 2. Major Existing Scales (Chronological)

### 2.1 SNAIL — Scale for the Assessment of Non-experts' AI Literacy

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Authors** | Laupichler, M. C., Aster, A., Perschewski, J.-O., & Schleiss, J. |
| **Year** | 2023 |
| **Journal** | Computers in Human Behavior Reports, 12, 100338 |
| **Items** | 31 items, 7-point Likert |
| **Factors** | 3 factors: Technical Understanding, Critical Appraisal, Practical Application |
| **Population** | Non-experts (no formal AI/CS education) |
| **Validation** | EFA; adequate reliability and validity |
| **Limitation** | No CFA confirmation; no behavioral validation |

**Relevance to AALS:**
- "Critical Appraisal" factor partially overlaps with AALS Subscale 3 (Calibration Agency)
- Does NOT address AI autonomy recognition or task-specific trust differentiation
- Potential convergent validity instrument for AALS

### 2.2 MAILS — Meta AI Literacy Scale

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Authors** | Carolus, A., Koch, M. J., et al. |
| **Year** | 2023 (original); 2024 (short version) |
| **Journal** | Computers in Human Behavior: Artificial Humans, 1(2) (original); Heliyon (short) |
| **Items** | 34 items (original); 10 items (short version) |
| **Factors** | AI Literacy (Create, Use, Know & Understand, Ethics, Detect AI) + Meta-competencies (AI Self-Competency, AI Self-Efficacy, Emotion Regulation, Persuasion Literacy, Problem Solving, Learning) |
| **Population** | Adults |
| **Validation** | CFA for short version; good model fit |
| **Unique Feature** | Includes psychological meta-competencies beyond AI knowledge |

**Relevance to AALS:**
- "Persuasion Literacy" (recognizing AI influence on decisions) conceptually related to AALS Calibration Agency
- "AI Self-Competency" and "AI Self-Efficacy" may serve as convergent/discriminant validity measures
- Does NOT address automation vs. agent distinction or task verifiability
- Short version (10 items) could be used as concurrent measure in AALS Study 2a

### 2.3 AILQ — AI Literacy Questionnaire (Ng et al.)

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Authors** | Ng, D. T. K., Wu, W., Leung, J. K. L., Chiu, T. K. F., & Chu, S. K. W. |
| **Year** | 2024 |
| **Journal** | British Journal of Educational Technology, 55(3), 1082–1104 |
| **Items** | 32 items, self-report |
| **Factors** | 4 dimensions (ABCE): Affective (motivation, self-efficacy), Behavioural (commitment, collaboration), Cognitive (know, understand, apply, evaluate, create), Ethical |
| **Population** | Secondary school students (Hong Kong) |
| **Validation** | Expert judgement, interviews, pilot study, 1st/2nd order CFA |
| **Framework** | ABCE (Affective-Behavioural-Cognitive-Ethical) learning approach |

**Relevance to AALS:**
- Comprehensive framework but focused on general AI literacy, not trust calibration
- "Cognitive: Evaluate" dimension partially overlaps with AALS critical evaluation
- Secondary school population; AALS targets higher education
- ABCE framework does not address system autonomy or task verifiability

### 2.4 SAIL4ALL — Scale of AI Literacy for All

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Authors** | Pinski, M., et al. |
| **Year** | 2025 |
| **Journal** | Humanities and Social Sciences Communications, 12 |
| **Items** | 56 items |
| **Factors** | 4 themes: (1) What is AI? (Recognizing AI, Understanding Intelligence), (2) What can AI do?, (3) How does AI work?, (4) How should AI be used? |
| **Response Format** | Dual: True/False + 5-point Likert |
| **Population** | All adult populations (UK samples) |
| **Validation** | CFA, measurement invariance (gender, education level) |
| **Unique Feature** | Performance-based (knowledge test), not self-assessment |

**Relevance to AALS:**
- "What is AI?" and "What can AI do?" could be relevant to AI Autonomy Recognition
- Performance-based format contrasts with AALS self-report approach
- Does NOT address trust, calibration, or task-specific evaluation
- 56 items is very long; AALS targets 12–18 items

## 3. Trust Measurement Instruments in AI

### 3.1 Trust in Automation Scale (TIAS) / S-TIAS

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Original** | 12 items (Jian et al., 2000) |
| **Short Version** | 3 items (S-TIAS, 2025) |
| **Focus** | General trust in automated systems |

**Relevance to AALS:**

## 4. AI Autonomy and Agency Frameworks

### 4.1 Anthropic — Measuring Agent Autonomy in Practice (2025)

- Analyzed millions of Claude Code interactions
- Finding: Users grant more autonomy with experience (20% → 40%+ auto-approve over 750 sessions)
- Five autonomy levels: Operator → Collaborator → Consultant → Approver → Observer
- **Relevance:** Empirical evidence for the AI Autonomy dimension of AALS framework

### 4.2 GovAI — Code-Based Autonomy Assessment (2025)

- Autonomy measured via code inspection without running agents
- Two attributes: **Impact** (consequence scope) and **Oversight** (human control level)
- **Relevance:** Impact × Oversight taxonomy parallels AI Autonomy × Task Verifiability in AALS

### 4.3 AI Agents vs. Agentic AI Taxonomy (2025)

- Three-tier distinction: Generative AI (reactive content) → AI Agents (tool-based tasks) → Agentic AI (multi-agent coordination)
- Agentic AI features: recursive task reallocation, cross-agent messaging, emergent decision-making
- **Relevance:** Supports the theoretical distinction in AALS Dimension 1

## 5. Gap Analysis: Why AALS is Needed

### 5.1 Dimension Coverage Matrix

| Existing Scale | AI Autonomy Recognition | Task-Specific Trust Differentiation | Calibration Agency | Trust Calibration |
|---------------|:---:|:---:|:---:|:---:|
| SNAIL (2023) | - | - | Partial (Critical Appraisal) | - |
| MAILS (2023) | - | - | Partial (Persuasion Literacy) | - |
| AILQ/ABCE (2024) | - | - | Partial (Cognitive: Evaluate) | - |
| SAIL4ALL (2025) | Partial (What is AI?) | - | - | - |
| GAIL variants (2025) | - | - | Partial (Content Evaluation) | - |
| Collab AI Metacog (2025) | - | - | Yes (Monitoring, Evaluation) | - |
| TIAS/S-TIAS | - | - | - | Partial (General trust) |
| HATAS (2024) | - | - | Partial (User Autonomy) | - |
| **AALS (proposed)** | **Yes** | **Yes** | **Yes** | **Yes** |

### 5.2 Unique Contributions of AALS

1. **AI Autonomy Recognition (AAR):** No existing scale measures learners' ability to distinguish automation from agent. SAIL4ALL touches "What is AI?" but at a general knowledge level, not the functional autonomy distinction.

2. **Task-Specific Trust Differentiation (TTD):** No existing scale measures whether learners adjust trust based on task verifiability. This is entirely novel.

3. **Calibration Agency (CA):** Several scales touch on critical evaluation/metacognition, but none connect it specifically to trust calibration in AI contexts. The Collaborative AI Metacognition Scale is closest but lacks the trust calibration framing.

4. **Three-Dimensional Integration:** No existing instrument combines all three dimensions into a unified framework that predicts trust calibration behavior.

### 5.3 Key Gaps in the Field

| Gap | Evidence | AALS Response |
|-----|----------|---------------|
| No automation-agent literacy measure | None of 16 scales in systematic review address this | Subscale 1 (AAR) |
| Trust ≠ Trust calibration | Trust scales measure attitude, not calibration accuracy | Framework distinguishes trust from calibration literacy |
| Task type ignored | All scales treat AI tasks as undifferentiated | Subscale 2 (TTD) |
| No cross-cultural validation | Systematic review finding: zero scales cross-culturally validated | Plan for Korean + English validation |
| Self-report dominance | Most scales only self-report | Study 2b behavioral validation |

## 7. Item Development Strategy

### 7.1 Sources for Item Pool

| Source Type | Specific Source | Subscale Relevance |
|-------------|----------------|-------------------|
| Theory: Wooldridge & Jennings (1995) | BDI agent model — autonomy, reactivity, proactivity | AAR |
| Theory: Parasuraman et al. (2000) | Levels of automation taxonomy | AAR |
| Theory: Hammond (1987) | Cognitive Continuum — intellective vs. judgmental | TTD |
| Theory: Bandura (2001) | Self-efficacy, agency | CA |
| Theory: Lee & See (2004) | Trust calibration, appropriate reliance | CA, TTD |
| Theory: Winne & Hadwin (1998) | SRL, metacognitive monitoring | CA |
| Scale: SNAIL Critical Appraisal items | Adapt for AI-specific evaluation | CA |
| Scale: MAILS Persuasion Literacy items | Adapt for trust calibration context | CA |
| Scale: Collab AI Metacognition | Adapt monitoring/evaluation items | CA |
| Empirical: Anthropic (2025) | Autonomy level descriptions | AAR |
| Empirical: GovAI (2025) | Impact × Oversight taxonomy | AAR, TTD |

### 7.2 Item Writing Guidelines

1. Frame items at the **construct level**, not technology-specific (avoid mentioning ChatGPT, Copilot, etc.)
2. Use **behavioral anchors** where possible ("When I use an AI tool, I..." rather than "I believe that...")
3. Maintain **reading level** appropriate for university students
4. Balance **positive and negative** wording (reverse-coded items ~30%)
5. Avoid **double-barreled** items
6. Test for **social desirability bias** through item wording

## 8. Summary and Next Steps

### What We Now Know
- 16+ AI literacy scales exist, none addressing trust calibration
- Closest relatives: Collaborative AI Metacognition Scale, MAILS Persuasion Literacy
- Theoretical frameworks for all three AALS dimensions are well-established
- Field lacks: automation-agent distinction measurement, task-specific trust differentiation, cross-cultural validation

### Immediate Next Steps
1. **Obtain full texts** of key scales: MAILS (short), Collab AI Metacognition, SNAIL, SAIL4ALL
2. **Extract and compare item pools** across scales for adaptation potential
3. **Draft initial AALS item pool** (40–45 items, ~15 per subscale)
4. **Recruit expert panel** (5–7 members: AI literacy, educational measurement, trust research, SRL)
5. **Plan cognitive interviews** (8–10 university students)
