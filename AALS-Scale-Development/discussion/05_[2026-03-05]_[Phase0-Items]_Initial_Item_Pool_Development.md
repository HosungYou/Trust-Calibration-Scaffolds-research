## 1. Item Development Strategy

### 1.1 Guiding Principles

1. **Process model alignment**: Each item maps to one of three process stages (Awareness → Judgment → Action)
2. **Self-report of readiness**: Items measure readiness/capacity, not actual calibration performance
3. **AI-context specificity**: Items refer to AI tool use in educational/learning contexts
4. **Behavioral anchoring**: Where possible, items describe observable behaviors or concrete situations
5. **Reverse-coded items**: ~25% reverse-coded to control acquiescence bias
6. **Reading level**: Accessible to undergraduate students (no jargon)

### 1.2 Response Format

| Subscale | Anchors | Rationale |
|----------|---------|-----------|
| CA-Aw (Awareness) | 1 = Strongly Disagree … 7 = Strongly Agree | Self-perception of awareness state |
| CA-Jd (Judgment) | 1 = Strongly Disagree … 7 = Strongly Agree | Self-perception of evaluative ability |
| CA-Ac (Action) | 1 = Never … 7 = Always | Behavioral frequency — reduces acquiescence bias |

### 1.3 Target Numbers

- **Initial pool**: 42 items (14 per subscale)
- **After expert panel (CVI)**: ~30-36 items
- **After EFA**: ~18-24 items (6-8 per subscale)
- **Final scale**: ~15-21 items (5-7 per subscale)

## 2. Item Pool

### Subscale 1: Calibration Awareness (CA-Aw)

**Process Stage:** Recognition — "Do I know how much I currently trust this AI?"
**Theoretical Basis:** Winne & Hadwin (1998) — self-monitoring in SRL; metacognitive awareness
**Includes:** (a) Trust state awareness, (b) AI system understanding, (c) Task context recognition

#### Trust State Awareness (knowing where you are)

| # | Item | Facet | Direction |
|---|------|-------|-----------|
| Aw01 | When I use an AI tool for a learning task, I am aware of how much I trust its output. | Trust state | + |
| Aw02 | I can recognize when I am relying too heavily on AI-generated content. | Trust state | + |
| Aw03 | I rarely think about whether I am trusting AI too much or too little. | Trust state | − (R) |
| Aw04 | I notice when my level of trust in an AI tool changes during a task. | Trust state | + |
| Aw05 | I pay attention to how confident I feel about AI-generated results. | Trust state | + |

#### AI System Understanding (knowing the AI's capabilities)

| # | Item | Facet | Direction |
|---|------|-------|-----------|
| Aw06 | Before trusting an AI tool's output, I consider what the AI was designed to do. | System understanding | + |
| Aw07 | I think about how an AI tool generates its responses when I use it for learning. | System understanding | + |
| Aw08 | I don't usually consider the limitations of an AI tool when using its suggestions. | System understanding | − (R) |
| Aw09 | I can tell the difference between AI tasks that require human judgment and those that don't. | System understanding | + |

#### Task Context Recognition (knowing the situation)

| # | Item | Facet | Direction |
|---|------|-------|-----------|
| Aw10 | I consider whether a task is suitable for AI assistance before relying on it. | Task context | + |
| Aw11 | I recognize that different types of tasks require different levels of trust in AI. | Task context | + |
| Aw12 | I treat AI outputs the same regardless of the type of task. | Task context | − (R) |
| Aw13 | When using AI for learning, I think about how important accuracy is for the specific task. | Task context | + |
| Aw14 | I am aware that AI tools perform differently depending on the subject area. | Task context | + |

**Total CA-Aw: 14 items (4 reverse-coded = 29%)**

### Subscale 2: Calibration Judgment (CA-Jd)

**Process Stage:** Evaluation — "Is my current trust level appropriate for this situation?"
**Theoretical Basis:** Lee & See (2004) — trust as attitude influencing reliance; Sperber et al. (2010) — epistemic vigilance
**Includes:** (a) Appropriateness evaluation, (b) Situational comparison, (c) Discrepancy detection

#### Appropriateness Evaluation (judging whether trust matches the situation)

| # | Item | Facet | Direction |
|---|------|-------|-----------|
| Jd01 | I can judge whether my level of trust in an AI tool is appropriate for a given task. | Appropriateness | + |
| Jd02 | I am able to evaluate when I should trust AI more and when I should trust it less. | Appropriateness | + |
| Jd03 | I find it difficult to know if I am trusting an AI tool the right amount. | Appropriateness | − (R) |
| Jd04 | I can assess whether an AI's response deserves my trust based on the task. | Appropriateness | + |
| Jd05 | I feel confident in my ability to determine when AI output is reliable enough to use. | Appropriateness | + |

#### Situational Comparison (evaluating trust relative to context)

| # | Item | Facet | Direction |
|---|------|-------|-----------|
| Jd06 | I compare AI-generated content with what I already know about a topic before deciding to trust it. | Comparison | + |
| Jd07 | When AI gives me an answer, I consider whether other sources would give similar results. | Comparison | + |
| Jd08 | I evaluate AI suggestions differently depending on how much is at stake in the task. | Comparison | + |
| Jd09 | I accept AI outputs without considering how well they fit the specific context. | Comparison | − (R) |

#### Discrepancy Detection (noticing when trust is misaligned)

| # | Item | Facet | Direction |
|---|------|-------|-----------|
| Jd10 | I can sense when something about an AI's output doesn't seem right, even if I can't explain why. | Discrepancy | + |
| Jd11 | I notice inconsistencies in AI-generated content that suggest it may be unreliable. | Discrepancy | + |
| Jd12 | I tend to miss errors in AI outputs unless someone else points them out. | Discrepancy | − (R) |
| Jd13 | When an AI tool gives an unexpected answer, I question whether the output is trustworthy. | Discrepancy | + |
| Jd14 | I can identify situations where trusting AI would lead to a poor outcome. | Discrepancy | + |

**Total CA-Jd: 14 items (3 reverse-coded = 21%)**

### Subscale 3: Calibration Action (CA-Ac)

**Process Stage:** Regulation — "Do I actively adjust my trust and verify AI outputs?"
**Theoretical Basis:** Bandura (2001) — human agency (intentionality, self-reactiveness, self-reflectiveness)
**Includes:** (a) Verification behaviors, (b) Trust adjustment, (c) Strategic regulation
**Response Format:** 1 = Never … 7 = Always

#### Verification Behaviors (checking AI outputs)

| # | Item | Facet | Direction |
|---|------|-------|-----------|
| Ac01 | I check AI-generated answers against other sources before using them. | Verification | + |
| Ac02 | I cross-reference important AI outputs with textbooks, articles, or other reliable sources. | Verification | + |
| Ac03 | I use AI suggestions without verifying them. | Verification | − (R) |
| Ac04 | When an AI tool gives me a factual claim, I look it up to confirm it is correct. | Verification | + |
| Ac05 | I ask follow-up questions to an AI tool to test the quality of its responses. | Verification | + |

#### Trust Adjustment (modifying reliance based on evidence)

| # | Item | Facet | Direction |
|---|------|-------|-----------|
| Ac06 | I increase or decrease my reliance on AI based on how well it has performed in similar tasks. | Adjustment | + |
| Ac07 | After finding an error in an AI output, I change how much I trust it for future tasks. | Adjustment | + |
| Ac08 | Even when AI gives me wrong answers, I continue to use it the same way. | Adjustment | − (R) |
| Ac09 | I adjust how much I depend on AI tools depending on the difficulty of the task. | Adjustment | + |

#### Strategic Regulation (deliberate calibration strategies)

| # | Item | Facet | Direction |
|---|------|-------|-----------|
| Ac10 | I have specific strategies for evaluating whether AI-generated content is trustworthy. | Strategy | + |
| Ac11 | I intentionally test AI tools on questions I already know the answer to. | Strategy | + |
| Ac12 | I set personal rules for when to accept and when to question AI outputs. | Strategy | + |
| Ac13 | I don't have a systematic way to evaluate AI outputs — I go with my gut feeling. | Strategy | − (R) |
| Ac14 | I reflect on whether my use of AI helped or hurt my learning after completing a task. | Strategy | + |

**Total CA-Ac: 14 items (3 reverse-coded = 21%)**

## 4. Theory-to-Item Mapping

### 4.1 Process Model Connection

```
METACOGNITION (Winne, 1998) ──→ Subscale 1: Calibration Awareness
  "Do I know how much I trust?"      Aw01–Aw14
  Self-monitoring of trust state       Trust state + System understanding + Task context
        ↓
TRUST (Lee & See, 2004) ──→ Subscale 2: Calibration Judgment
  "Is this trust appropriate?"         Jd01–Jd14
  Evaluating trust-reliability match   Appropriateness + Comparison + Discrepancy
        ↓
AGENCY (Bandura, 2001) ──→ Subscale 3: Calibration Action
  "Do I actively adjust?"              Ac01–Ac14
  Intentional regulation behavior      Verification + Adjustment + Strategy
        ↓
CALIBRATION (Outcome) ──→ NOT directly measured (readiness, not accuracy)
```

### 4.2 Cross-Reference with Existing Scales

| Existing Scale | Related Items | Expected Relationship |
|---------------|---------------|----------------------|
| MAILS (AI literacy) | Aw06, Aw07, Aw08 | Convergent (r = .40-.60) — AI knowledge overlaps |
| CT Disposition (Sosu, 2013) | Jd06, Jd07, Jd10, Jd11 | Convergent but distinct (r = .30-.50) — **TTD falsification test** |
| S-TIAS (trust attitude) | Aw01, Aw02, Jd01 | Discriminant (r < .30) — trust state ≠ trust attitude |
| MAI (metacognitive awareness) | Aw01-Aw05, Ac10, Ac14 | Convergent (r = .40-.60) — metacognition overlap |
| General Self-Efficacy | Jd05, Ac10 | Discriminant (r < .30) — calibration readiness ≠ general confidence |

## 5. Potential Issues to Address in Expert Review

1. **Aw09 / Jd08 overlap**: Both address task-type differentiation — expert panel should judge if both are needed
2. **Jd05 may load on self-efficacy**: "I feel confident in my ability..." could correlate highly with general self-efficacy
3. **Ac05 is AI-chatbot specific**: "ask follow-up questions" may not apply to all AI tools (e.g., grammar checkers)
4. **Ac11 may be niche behavior**: "test AI on known questions" — do learners actually do this?
5. **CA-Aw and CA-Jd boundary**: Several items (Aw09, Aw10, Aw11) could cross-load with Jd items — EFA will decide
6. **Double-barreled items**: Check Aw09 ("require human judgment AND those that don't") — may need splitting

## 6. Next Steps

1. **Expert panel review (CVI)**: 5-7 experts rate each item for relevance (1-4 scale), calculate I-CVI and S-CVI
2. **Cognitive interviews**: 8-10 learners think aloud while answering — identify confusing items
3. **Item revision**: Revise based on CVI + cognitive interview feedback
4. **Pilot test**: Small sample (N=50-80) for initial reliability check
5. **Study 1 (EFA)**: N=300-350, determine factor structure empirically
