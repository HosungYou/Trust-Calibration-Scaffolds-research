# Conceptual Paper Detailed Design

**Paper Title:** Trust Calibration Readiness as an Educational Competency: Integrating Calibration Science with AI Literacy Frameworks
**Paper Type:** Conceptual / Theoretical
**Target:** Educational Psychologist or Learning and Instruction

---

## 1. Rationale and Gap Justification

### 1.1 Why This Paper Is Needed

The rapid integration of AI into education has created a new pedagogical challenge: learners must not only use AI tools but trust them *appropriately*. Bastani et al. (2025) demonstrated that unrestricted AI tutoring access produced 17% lower performance on unassisted assessments, attributable to uncalibrated overtrust and cognitive offloading. Yet no existing educational framework identifies the capacity to calibrate one's trust in AI as a distinct, teachable competency.

Two scholarly traditions study calibration but remain disconnected:

| Tradition | Core Question | Key Scholars |
|-----------|---------------|-------------|
| **Calibration science** (educational psychology) | "How accurately do learners judge their own knowledge?" | Alexander (2013), Bol & Hacker (2012), Dunlosky & Rawson (2012) |
| **Trust in automation** (human factors) | "How well does a user's trust match system reliability?" | Lee & See (2004), Hoff & Bashir (2015), Wischnewski et al. (2023) |

Meanwhile, AI literacy frameworks (Long & Magerko, 2020; Ng et al., 2021; Laupichler et al., 2022) address what learners should *know* about AI but not how learners should *regulate their trust* in AI. This paper bridges these three bodies of work.

### 1.2 Core Thesis

> **Trust calibration readiness** — the capacity to monitor, evaluate, and regulate one's trust in AI relative to the AI's actual reliability — should be recognized as a distinct educational competency within AI literacy frameworks.

### 1.3 Unique Contributions

| Contribution | Description |
|-------------|-------------|
| **C1: Cross-disciplinary bridge** | First paper to formally integrate calibration science (Alexander, 2013) with trust-in-automation theory (Lee & See, 2004) into an educational competency |
| **C2: Process model as competency** | Reframes the Awareness → Judgment → Action process model as a teachable, assessable educational competency |
| **C3: AI literacy gap identification** | Systematically demonstrates that existing AI literacy frameworks omit trust calibration |
| **C4: Developmental framework** | Proposes developmental progression from novice to expert calibrator |
| **C5: Dual-referent construct** | Distinguishes trust calibration readiness from metacognitive awareness through its relational (self + AI) structure |

## 2. Paper Structure (7 Sections)

### Section 1: The Calibration Imperative in the Age of AI

**Purpose:** Establish the problem — learners need trust calibration, but no framework treats it as an educational competency.

**Key arguments:**

1. AI tools are now pervasive in education (ChatGPT, Copilot, AI tutors, writing assistants)
2. Evidence that uncalibrated trust harms learning:
   - Bastani et al. (2025): 17% performance drop with unrestricted AI access
   - Cognitive offloading (Risko & Gilbert, 2016): reduced effortful processing
   - Automation bias (Goddard et al., 2012): uncritical acceptance of AI outputs
3. Trust is not binary — the goal is not more or less trust, but *calibrated* trust (Lee & See, 2004)
4. **Thesis statement:** Trust calibration readiness is a distinct educational competency that requires explicit conceptualization, instruction, and assessment.

**Opening paragraph direction:**

> When a learner asks ChatGPT to explain photosynthesis, the quality of learning depends not only on the accuracy of the AI's response but on the learner's capacity to judge that accuracy. A learner who accepts the explanation uncritically learns less than one who evaluates it against prior knowledge and seeks corroboration — even when the AI's response is correct (Bastani et al., 2025). This capacity to calibrate one's trust in AI is not a byproduct of general intelligence or digital fluency; it is a specific competency that can be developed through instruction. Yet no existing educational framework identifies it as such.

### Section 2: Calibration Science — Two Traditions

**Purpose:** Systematically compare two calibration traditions and establish a theoretical bridge.

#### 2.1 Metacognitive Calibration (Educational Psychology)

**Alexander (2013) — Foundational Definition:**

> Calibration is "the degree to which individuals' judgments about their understanding, capability, competence, or **preparedness** correspond to what they actually manifest."

Two foundational questions (Alexander, 2013):
- **Q1:** What is the nature and bases of calibration judgments?
- **Q2:** Under what conditions are calibration judgments accurate?

**Key features of the tradition:**

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Epistemic target** | Self — "How well do I know this?" |
| **Reference standard** | Own performance on a task |
| **Measurement** | Confidence-accuracy correspondence (gamma, bias, absolute accuracy) |
| **Process** | Monitoring → judgment → regulation (Nelson & Narens, 1990) |
| **Theoretical home** | Metacognition, self-regulated learning |
| **Key scholars** | Alexander (2013), Bol & Hacker (2012), Dunlosky & Rawson (2012), Dinsmore & Parkinson (2013), Schraw (2009) |

**Bol & Hacker (2012) six research directions:**

1. Domain specificity of calibration
2. Developmental trajectory
3. Calibration training interventions
4. Relationship to SRL
5. Measurement precision
6. Individual differences

**None of these six directions addresses trust in external systems.**

#### 2.2 Trust Calibration (Human Factors)

**Lee & See (2004) — Foundational Definition:**

> Trust calibration is "how well trust matches the true capabilities of the automation."

**Key features of the tradition:**

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Epistemic target** | System — "How reliable is this AI?" |
| **Reference standard** | AI system's actual performance |
| **Measurement** | Trust-reliability gap, appropriate reliance rate |
| **Process** | Trust formation → reliance decision → outcome feedback |
| **Theoretical home** | Trust in automation, human factors |
| **Key scholars** | Lee & See (2004), Hoff & Bashir (2015), de Visser et al. (2020), Wischnewski et al. (2023) |

**Wischnewski et al. (2023) — Current State:**

- Survey of 96 trust calibration studies
- Trust calibration measured as group-level difference in trust across high/low reliability conditions
- Conclusion: "the measurement of the trust calibration often limits the interpretation of the effects of different interventions"
- No individual-level process measure exists

#### 2.3 The Bridge — Alexander's "Preparedness" as Theoretical Warrant

**Comparative Analysis:**

| Dimension | Metacognitive Calibration | Trust Calibration | Trust Calibration Readiness (Proposed) |
|-----------|--------------------------|-------------------|---------------------------------------|
| **What is calibrated?** | Self-knowledge accuracy | Trust-reliability correspondence | Readiness to achieve trust-reliability correspondence |
| **Epistemic target** | Self | External system | Self-in-relation-to-system (dual-referent) |
| **Reference standard** | Own performance | AI reliability | Both (trust state + AI reliability) |
| **Level of analysis** | Individual | Group (experimental) | Individual |
| **Measurement** | Confidence-performance gap | Trust-reliability gap | Self-report process assessment |
| **Alexander's Q1** | "What are my knowledge judgments based on?" | N/A (not addressed) | "What are my trust judgments based on?" |
| **Alexander's Q2** | "When are my self-judgments accurate?" | "When does trust match reliability?" | "Am I ready to make accurate trust judgments?" |
| **Core process** | Monitor → judge → regulate (self) | Form trust → decide reliance | Monitor trust → evaluate gap → adjust behavior |
| **Tradition** | Educational psychology | Human factors | **Cross-disciplinary** |

**The bridge argument:**

Alexander (2013) defines calibration broadly enough to include "preparedness" — which is precisely what trust calibration readiness measures. Her Q1 (nature and bases of judgments) maps directly onto the Awareness and Judgment stages of the process model. The proposed construct extends Alexander's framework from self-directed calibration to system-directed calibration, maintaining the same metacognitive architecture (monitoring → evaluation → regulation) while redirecting the epistemic target from self to AI system.

This is not a metaphorical extension but a structural one: the same cognitive processes that enable a learner to judge "How well do I understand this topic?" can be redirected to judge "How reliable is this AI for this task?" — provided the learner has the domain knowledge, AI literacy, and metacognitive disposition to make that judgment.

#### 3.3 Why Existing Frameworks Cannot Absorb Calibration

Three structural reasons:

1. **Knowledge ≠ Process:** Knowing AI has limitations (AI literacy) ≠ recognizing when YOUR trust exceeds AI reliability (calibration awareness). The former is declarative knowledge; the latter is metacognitive monitoring.

2. **General critical thinking ≠ Trust-specific calibration:** Critical evaluation of AI outputs can occur without any awareness of one's own trust state. A learner might critically evaluate a specific output while remaining generally overtrusting of the system. Calibration requires the relational judgment: "My trust level relative to this AI's reliability."

3. **Output evaluation ≠ Self-regulation:** Evaluating whether an AI response is correct (output focus) differs from adjusting one's own reliance behavior based on calibration assessment (self-regulation focus). The latter requires metacognitive control, not just cognitive analysis.

#### 4.2 Stage-by-Stage Educational Specification

**Stage 1: Calibration Awareness (CA-Aw)**

| Dimension | Specification |
|-----------|--------------|
| **Definition** | The ability to recognize one's own trust state (level, basis, and direction) in relation to an AI system's reliability |
| **Cognitive process** | Metacognitive monitoring directed at trust judgments |
| **Dual-referent structure** | Requires simultaneous awareness of (a) own trust level AND (b) AI system reliability signals |
| **Prerequisite knowledge** | Basic AI literacy — understanding that AI has variable reliability across tasks and domains |
| **Observable indicators** | Can articulate: "I trust this AI [high/medium/low] for this task because [reasons]" |
| **Failure mode** | Trust without awareness — the learner has a trust level but cannot articulate or examine it |
| **Instructional strategy** | Trust reflection prompts before AI interaction; trust journaling; explicit trust-rating exercises |
| **Assessment** | Self-report items using monitoring verbs: "I notice," "I am aware," "I pay attention to" |

**Stage 2: Calibration Judgment (CA-Jd)**

| Dimension | Specification |
|-----------|--------------|
| **Definition** | The ability to evaluate whether one's trust level is proportionate to the AI system's actual reliability for a specific task |
| **Cognitive process** | Evaluative comparison — internal trust state vs. external reliability evidence |
| **Dual-referent structure** | Requires judgment about the RELATIONSHIP between trust and reliability, not either in isolation |
| **Prerequisite** | Stage 1 (Awareness) — cannot evaluate alignment without first recognizing trust state |
| **Observable indicators** | Can articulate: "My trust is [too high / appropriate / too low] for this AI on this task because [evidence]" |
| **Failure mode** | Awareness without judgment — the learner knows their trust level but cannot assess its appropriateness |
| **Instructional strategy** | Error detection tasks; AI output comparison exercises; reliability information provision |
| **Assessment** | Self-report items using evaluative verbs: "I evaluate," "I weigh," "I compare" |

**Stage 3: Calibration Action (CA-Ac)**

| Dimension | Specification |
|-----------|--------------|
| **Definition** | The ability to deliberately adjust reliance behavior based on calibration judgment |
| **Cognitive process** | Metacognitive control — volitional regulation of trust-driven behavior |
| **Dual-referent structure** | Action is directed at REDUCING the trust-reliability gap (not just changing behavior) |
| **Prerequisite** | Stage 2 (Judgment) — cannot adjust appropriately without evaluating the gap |
| **Observable indicators** | Increases verification when overtrusting; increases reliance when undertrusting; adjusts strategy based on task-specific reliability |
| **Failure mode** | Judgment without action — the learner recognizes miscalibration but does not adjust behavior |
| **Instructional strategy** | Verification scaffolds; progressive autonomy release; behavioral self-monitoring |
| **Assessment** | Self-report items using action/frequency verbs: "I verify," "I adjust," "I change my approach" |

#### 4.3 Sequential Structure Justification

The three stages are proposed as *sequentially dependent* but not strictly linear:

```
Awareness ──prerequisite──▶ Judgment ──prerequisite──▶ Action
                                                         │
                              ◀────── feedback ──────────┘
```

**Arguments for sequential dependency:**

1. **Logical prerequisite:** One cannot evaluate trust-reliability alignment (Judgment) without first recognizing one's trust state (Awareness). One cannot adjust behavior strategically (Action) without first identifying the direction of miscalibration (Judgment).

2. **Theoretical precedent:** Nelson & Narens (1990) model monitoring as preceding control. Zimmerman (2000) models forethought → performance → self-reflection as sequential phases. Alexander's (2013) Q1 (nature of judgments) logically precedes Q2 (conditions for accuracy).

3. **Educational implication:** Instruction should follow the sequence — build awareness before training judgment, train judgment before scaffolding action. This has direct curriculum design implications.

**Against strict linearity:**

4. Feedback from Action informs subsequent Awareness — creating a cycle, not a one-way path.
5. Expert calibrators may compress or automatize stages, making the process appear non-sequential.
6. Some interventions (e.g., productive failure) may activate multiple stages simultaneously.

#### 6.2 Connection to Alexander's Model of Domain Learning (MDL)

Alexander's (1997, 2003) MDL describes how learners progress through three phases of domain expertise: **Acclimation** → **Competence** → **Proficiency**. Key MDL principles applicable to trust calibration readiness:

| MDL Principle | Application to Trust Calibration Readiness |
|--------------|-------------------------------------------|
| **Knowledge matters** | Domain knowledge AND AI knowledge are prerequisites for calibration; without understanding what AI can/cannot do, trust judgments lack an informed basis |
| **Interest develops** | Initial individual interest in AI may draw learners in; sustained situational interest in calibration develops through meaningful, consequential AI interactions |
| **Strategic processing deepens** | Novices use surface strategies (blanket trust/distrust); proficient calibrators use deep strategies (task-specific, evidence-based assessment) |
| **Phases are not stages** | Progression is gradual and domain-specific; a learner may be proficient at calibrating trust in AI writing tools but novice at calibrating trust in AI math tutors |

**This parallel is the strongest connection point for Alexander collaboration:** her MDL framework provides the developmental architecture that trust calibration readiness currently lacks. Inviting her to develop this section would produce a genuinely integrative contribution.

#### 6.3 Developmental Hypotheses (Testable)

| ID | Hypothesis | Derived From |
|----|-----------|-------------|
| H1 | Trust calibration readiness improves with repeated AI interaction + feedback | Learned trust layer (Hoff & Bashir, 2015) |
| H2 | Domain expertise moderates trust calibration: experts calibrate faster in their domain | MDL knowledge principle (Alexander, 2003) |
| H3 | Metacognitive instruction accelerates calibration development | Metacognitive scaffolding evidence (Guo et al., 2022) |
| H4 | Calibration readiness transfers across AI systems within the same task domain | MDL domain-specificity |
| H5 | Calibration readiness transfer across domains is limited without explicit bridging instruction | MDL phase specificity |
| H6 | The Awareness → Judgment sequence develops before the Judgment → Action sequence | Nelson & Narens (1990) monitoring precedes control |

### Section 7: Implications and Research Agenda

**Purpose:** Derive actionable implications and a priority research agenda.

#### 7.1 Implications for Curriculum Design

| Implication | Detail |
|------------|--------|
| **I1: Integrate TCR into AI literacy curricula** | TCR should be a named competency alongside technical AI knowledge and ethical awareness |
| **I2: Sequence instruction following the process model** | Teach awareness → judgment → action in order; do not assume awareness develops automatically |
| **I3: Provide reliability information** | Learners cannot calibrate without access to AI reliability information; curricula should include exercises comparing AI performance across tasks |
| **I4: Use scaffolded AI interactions** | Structured AI encounters with embedded calibration prompts, rather than unstructured AI access |
| **I5: Assess calibration readiness, not just AI knowledge** | Include process-based assessment of monitoring, evaluation, and behavioral regulation capacity |

#### 7.2 Implications for Teacher Preparation
